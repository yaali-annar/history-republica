# Maximilien Robespierre (1758–1823)

**Deputy of the National Assembly; President of the Committee of Public Accounts**  
Born: Arras, France, 6 May 1758  
Died: Paris, 17 March 1823

---

## Overview

Maximilien Robespierre is remembered in the French Republic as the man who made its accounts honest. He ordered no executions. He held no military command. He lived in a small apartment in the Rue Saint-Honoré and owned almost nothing and worked until exhaustion took him, and he left behind a republic that was measurably cleaner for his thirty years of attention to it. The epithet that attached itself to him during the Revolution — the Incorruptible — proved, in his case, to be accurate in the most literal sense: he simply could not be bought, and he found, to his evident satisfaction, that the same expectation applied to everyone else.

## Early Career

Robespierre trained as a lawyer in Arras, where he took cases on behalf of clients who could not afford the fees he declined to charge. His early reputation was for thoroughness and a certain humorless precision that won arguments and did not win friends. He was elected to the Estates-General in 1789 as a deputy for the Third Estate of Artois, and arrived in Versailles with positions he had already worked out and no intention of modifying them for social reasons.

The Revolution confirmed his instincts without requiring him to become a different person. He was a significant voice from the earliest sessions — consistent, prepared, principled in the specific sense of having principles and applying them without exception.

## The Committee of Public Accounts

The crisis that might have made Robespierre the dominant figure of a revolutionary tribunal did not arrive. The French Republic's founding was less desperate than it might have been: the Maritime League's backing, the example of functioning republics on three sides, and the exile rather than execution of Louis Capet all denied the radical factions the siege mentality that feeds on emergency. What arrived instead was the enormous administrative problem of building a republic from the wreckage of an absolute monarchy — decades of court patronage to unwind, ecclesiastical property to account for, noble exemptions to abolish, and a civil service whose relationship with the public treasury had been, under the old regime, frankly informal.

Robespierre was given the Committee of Public Accounts and applied himself to it as though it were a personal matter, which in some sense it was. The committee's work was unglamorous and technically demanding and produced, over fifteen years, a transformation in the French Republic's fiscal administration that contemporaries were too close to fully appreciate. Robespierre himself was not interested in appreciation. He was interested in the numbers.

He published his findings. This was not standard practice. He made it standard practice, arguing in the National Assembly that a republic whose finances were not legible to its citizens was a republic whose finances were not under citizen control. The public ledger, complete and honest, became a constitutional requirement during his tenure. The officials who had hoped to continue the old regime's creative accounting under a new flag found themselves the subjects of investigations that Robespierre conducted with a thoroughness they had not anticipated and could not interrupt.

## The Incorruptible in Practice

Robespierre's personal finances were inspected, by his own invitation, on four separate occasions during his public career. The inspections found the same thing each time: the accounts of a man who had been paid a public salary for thirty years and spent it on rent, books, and the maintenance of a wardrobe that contemporaries described, uniformly, as clean but not remarkable.

He refused gifts. He refused fees. He refused the various courtesies that the old regime had normalised as perquisites of office. He did so without moral lecture — he simply did not accept them and moved on, which his colleagues found more unsettling than any speech.

His relationship with Napoleon Bonaparte was one of mutual recognition and persistent friction. Napoleon understood what Robespierre was for and was careful, in his presence, to be straightforwardly honest about his Italian accounts. Robespierre, for his part, found Napoleon's political restlessness tiresome and said so in committee when the occasion arose. They were useful to each other precisely because neither could be accused of accommodation.

## The Bernadotte Audit

In the years following Jean-Baptiste Bernadotte's acceptance of the Swedish High Guardianship, Napoleon Bonaparte raised public objections to the arrangement — principally that Bernadotte had accepted a quasi-monarchical position and its attendant material support without accountability to the republic whose officer he had been.

Bernadotte's response was a letter to Robespierre requesting a full audit of his Swedish accounts, his French accounts, and every financial arrangement between himself and the Swedish state since his arrival in Stockholm. The letter noted, without elaboration, that the person making the accusations might have preferred a different auditor.

Robespierre accepted without delay. He spent six weeks in Stockholm with access to every ledger Bernadotte could produce, which was every ledger that existed. His published report ran to forty pages. Its conclusion was that Bernadotte had accepted the maintenance necessary to perform his functions and nothing beyond it; that his personal accounts reflected a career of military service and no other income; and that the Swedish state had received from him considerably more than it had provided.

The report was read into the record of the National Assembly by three deputies, two of whom had previously been sympathetic to Napoleon's position. Napoleon attended the session and did not speak.

Robespierre returned to Paris and resumed his work at the Committee of Public Accounts. He did not comment publicly on the episode beyond the report itself. When asked, in a recorded conversation from this period, what he made of Bernadotte's arrangement with Sweden, he said he had made what the numbers showed, and the numbers had been clear.

## Final Years and Legacy

Robespierre left the Committee of Public Accounts in 1818, at sixty, citing the condition of his health. He died in Paris in 1823, in the apartment he had occupied for thirty years, and was buried in the plain wooden coffin he had specified in a document filed with a notary in 1791 and apparently never revised.

He left no wealth, no family, and no political faction. He left behind the public ledger requirement, a fiscal administration that his successors spent twenty years discovering they could not easily corrupt, and a republic that was, in measurable ways, more honest than it had been when he arrived.

The Incorruptible is not a popular figure in the French Republic's national mythology — he is admired rather than loved, and even the admiration has a quality of discomfort to it, as though the republic he served is not entirely sure it deserves him. Abroad, he is understood more simply. In the English Republic, in the Dutch Republic, in the Union of States, he is the name that stands for the proposition that a republic's accounts and its principles are the same thing.
