# Maximilien Robespierre (1758–1823)

**Premier Président de la Cour des Comptes de la République Française**  
Born: Arras, France, 6 May 1758  
Died: Paris, 17 March 1823

---

## Overview

Maximilien Robespierre is remembered in the French Republic as the man who made its accounts honest. He ordered no executions. He held no military command. He spent thirty years making corrupt officials wish they had chosen a different career, and he did it from a small apartment in the Rue Saint-Honoré that he occupied from his arrival in Paris until the morning he did not wake up. He owned almost nothing. He worked every day, including Septidi, including the days colleagues sent word that he really ought to rest. He left behind a republic that was measurably cleaner for his presence in it, and a body of public ledgers that his successors spent twenty years discovering they could not falsify.

Government officials hated him. The people did not.

## Early Career

Robespierre trained as a lawyer in Arras, where he took cases on behalf of clients who could not afford fees he declined to charge. He was appointed, early in his career, to judge cases involving injured birds and drainage disputes — the local magistracy's way of occupying a young lawyer whose thoroughness had already made several of his seniors uncomfortable. He was elected to the Estates-General in 1789 as a deputy for the Third Estate of Artois, and arrived in Versailles with positions already worked out and no intention of modifying them for social reasons.

The Revolution confirmed his instincts without requiring him to become a different person. He was a significant voice from the earliest sessions — prepared, consistent, and immune to the social pressure that the National Assembly exerted on people who wanted to be liked.

## The Cour des Comptes

The crisis that might have made Robespierre the dominant figure of a revolutionary tribunal did not arrive. The French Republic's founding was less desperate than it might have been — the Maritime League's backing, the example of functioning republics on three sides, and the exile rather than execution of Louis Capet all denied the radical factions the emergency they feed on. What arrived instead was the enormous administrative problem of building a republic from the wreckage of an absolute monarchy, including decades of court patronage to unwind and a civil service whose relationship with the public treasury had been, under the old regime, frankly informal.

The Cour des Comptes de la République Française was established in 1791 as an independent judicial body charged with auditing all public accounts and issuing binding financial judgments against officials found short. The model drew on Dutch fiscal oversight practices and on the English Republic's tradition of parliamentary account-giving; the specifically French addition was the court's authority to refer criminal matters directly to prosecution without requiring the approval of the ministries being investigated.

Robespierre became its first Premier Président at thirty-three. He held the position until his death.

The Cour's authority covered every public account in the republic: ministries, municipalities, departments, and — critically — military finances. Its findings were published in full. This last requirement was Robespierre's own insistence, and it was the source of both his popular reputation and the specific quality of loathing that senior officials developed for him. A minister who had been quietly redirecting departmental funds could weather an internal investigation. He could not easily weather a published report in language clear enough for any literate person to understand.

## The Work

Robespierre's method was not theatrical. He did not make speeches about corruption or invite the public galleries to witness dramatic confrontations. He read the ledgers. He asked for documents. He noted discrepancies with the precision of a man who genuinely enjoyed arithmetic and found its violations personally offensive. When the discrepancies were explained unsatisfactorily, he issued a formal arrêt de débet — a binding judgment requiring restitution — and referred the matter to prosecution if the amounts warranted it.

The officials who hated him most were not those he had prosecuted but those who had to prepare their accounts knowing he might read them. The Cour's reach was wide enough that even officials who had never been investigated changed their behavior on the expectation that they might be.

He received visitors at the apartment on Rue Saint-Honoré every day the Cour was not in session, and on several days it was. He answered correspondence personally. He worked on Septidi — the weekly day of rest — and on the days colleagues sent word that the republic would survive a brief holiday. It would not have been accurate to say that Robespierre rested on any particular day. It was accurate to say that when he was not reading accounts, he was writing findings about them.

## Napoleon Bonaparte and the Italian Accounts

The French Republic's military in northern Italy was, in the 1790s, conducting a campaign of considerable political complexity under general officers who were not uniformly scrupulous with regimental funds. Reports reached the Cour in the early years of the campaign. Robespierre reviewed them and requested a meeting with the officer commanding the theater.

The meeting, recorded in fragmentary notes by an aide, lasted two hours. Napoleon Bonaparte arrived expecting a politician and found something he had not previously encountered: a man who had read every financial report from the Italian theater and remembered the figures, who asked questions without interest in the answers Napoleon had prepared, and who made clear, without raising his voice, that the regimental accounts would be audited whether or not the Italian campaign was going well.

Napoleon's response, as recorded by the aide, was to answer the questions accurately. He confirmed two officers whose accounts were irregular, provided the documentation the Cour required, and expressed the view — not for publication — that Robespierre was an intensely difficult man to deal with and precisely what a republic that had just escaped from one form of corruption could not afford to be without.

The two officers were prosecuted. The Italian campaign continued. Robespierre sent no further representatives to the theater, having satisfied himself that the commanding general would submit honest accounts going forward.

## The Bernadotte Audit

The Cour's reputation, by the time Bernadotte invited Robespierre to Stockholm, was firmly established. Ministers had been prosecuted. A deputy had resigned before his findings were published. Three municipal administrators in Lyon had paid restitution totaling more than their combined salaries for the previous decade. The invitation from Bernadotte was received, in French government circles, with the particular silence of people who understood exactly what it meant.

Bernadotte's letter requested a full audit of his personal accounts, his arrangements with the Swedish state, and every financial transaction between himself and the crown since his arrival in Stockholm. He noted that he had insisted, as a condition of accepting the High Guardianship, on receiving a fixed annual salary in lieu of any access to state funds — a number agreed in advance and on the public record. He invited Robespierre to compare that number against his personal accounts and to examine what Sweden owned versus what he owned.

Robespierre spent six weeks in Stockholm. His published report was forty pages. Its findings were straightforward: the assets associated with the High Guardian's function — the residence, furnishings, staff, carriages, and the considerable material apparatus of a head of state — belonged to Sweden and were accounted for in Swedish state ledgers to which Bernadotte had no personal claim. Bernadotte's own assets were modest: a soldier's savings accumulated over thirty years of military salary, a small collection of books, and personal effects of no particular value. The Swedish salary, set at a number he had insisted on rather than negotiated, explained his personal accounts entirely. There was no remainder.

Napoleon Bonaparte, whose public objections to the High Guardianship arrangement had provided the occasion for the audit, attended the National Assembly session at which the report was read into the record. He did not speak.

## Death and Legacy

Robespierre died in Paris on 17 March 1823, at sixty-four. The cause, recorded by his physician, was exhaustion — a word that meant, in his case, exactly what it said. He had not been ill. He had simply used himself up.

He was buried in the plain wooden coffin he had specified in a document filed with a notary in 1791, which his heirs found on his desk, apparently revisited and reconfirmed in every decade since. The funeral was a public occasion that surprised no one more than the officials who had spent thirty years hoping he would retire. The streets of Paris were full.

He left no wealth, no property beyond the apartment's contents, and no political faction. He left behind the Cour des Comptes, the public ledger requirement, and the institutional expectation — imperfectly maintained by his successors but never entirely abandoned — that public accounts are public.

In the French Republic's national mythology he occupies the uncomfortable position of a man who was right in ways that required constant effort to acknowledge. Government officials, as a professional class, have never fully forgiven him. The people, who read the reports and understood what they said, have not forgotten who wrote them.

Abroad he is understood more simply. In the English Republic, the Dutch Republic, and the Union of States, his name is shorthand for the proposition that a republic's accounts and its principles are the same thing — and that someone has to check them.
