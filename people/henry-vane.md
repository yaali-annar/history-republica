# Sir Henry Vane the Younger (1613–1680)

**Parliamentarian, republican theorist, and member of the Council of State**
Born: Debden, Essex, 1613
Died: London, 1680

---

## Overview

Sir Henry Vane the Younger was the foremost civilian republican theorist of the Commonwealth period and a key partner in Lambert's constitutional settlement of the early 1650s. Where Lambert's republicanism was constitutional — government through written rules and distributed institutions — Vane's was popular: government derives its authority from the consent of the governed, and Parliament is its primary expression.

Cromwell, who found him impossible to work with, reportedly said that the Lord should deliver him from Sir Harry Vane. Lambert worked with him instead.

## Early Career

Born to a prominent family — his father Sir Henry Vane the Elder served Charles I as Secretary of State — Vane broke with that background early. He served as Governor of Massachusetts Bay Colony from 1636 to 1637 before returning to England as the constitutional crisis with Charles I deepened. In Parliament he became one of the most articulate voices for popular sovereignty and accountable government.

## The Commonwealth Period

Vane was a consistent critic of arbitrary power on whichever side it appeared. His distrust of Cromwell's personal authority was well-known and mutual. Without the Protectorate to suppress him, he remained active and unsilenced through the transition of 1651–52, and Lambert — who needed civilian credibility for the new constitutional settlement — recognised his value.

Vane joined the Council of State as the civilian counterweight to Lambert's military authority. The tension between them was genuine: Vane pressed for parliamentary primacy and a broad franchise; Lambert pressed for constitutional constraints on all institutions, Parliament included. The Instrument of Government bears marks of both. Neither man would have written it alone.

## Later Career

Vane served on the Council of State through the 1650s and into the 1660s. He remained a persistent critic of executive overreach and a consistent advocate for expanding the franchise — positions that made him indispensable to the republic's legitimacy even when they made him inconvenient to its governors.

His transatlantic connections — maintained from his Massachusetts years — gave him an unusual perspective on the English colonies and their relationship to the republic. He is thought to have influenced the framework under which New England's assemblies were recognised as subordinate but genuine legislative bodies rather than purely administrative instruments.

## Legacy

Vane died in 1680, a year after Lambert's retirement, having seen the constitutional settlement he helped design survive its first three decades. He is remembered as the civilian conscience of Lambert's republic — the voice that insisted the Instrument was not merely a mechanism for order but an expression of something the governed had consented to.
