# Republic of Mexico

**Official name**: Republic of Mexico  
**Capital**: Mexico City  
**Founded**: circa 1830s  
**Territory**: From the Rio Bravo del Norte and the California coast in the north, through Mesoamerica, to the Darién in the south  
**League membership**: League of Nations, 1907

---

## Overview

The Republic of Mexico is a federal republic occupying the southern breadth of the North American continent and the full length of the Mesoamerican isthmus, from its borders with Nouvelle Gaule and the Northwest Confederation in the north to the Darién gap where the continent narrows toward Gran Colombia. By territory it is one of the largest states in the League of Nations; by population, among the more substantial in the Americas.

Mexico's independence did not arrive through a decisive military campaign or a single founding document produced under fire. It arrived through a compound of Iberian strategic overextension, sustained pressure from independence movements that had been suppressed and revived across two decades, and the covert support of northern neighbors who calculated — correctly — that a republican Mexico suited their interests better than a continued Iberian presence on their southern flank. What it produced was a republic founded by negotiation rather than military decision, which has shaped its political character in ways that persist into the twentieth century.

---

## Colonial Heritage

New Spain was among the most valued of Iberia's colonial territories: wealthy, populous, and strategically positioned between Iberian Caribbean holdings and the distant Pacific. The viceregal capital at Mexico City was a genuinely metropolitan centre — more densely populated than most Iberian cities, sustaining universities, printing houses, and a creole intellectual culture that drew selectively on Enlightenment currents while developing its own terms.

The tensions that accumulated within this society were familiar in their outlines: a creole elite that governed locally but remained administratively subordinate to peninsular appointees; an indigenous population that formed the majority in most territories and whose relationship to colonial institutions ranged from accommodation to persistent low-level resistance; a mixed-race population positioned variously between these. Iberia's tightening of metropolitan control in the late eighteenth century — at the same moment that republican models were proliferating across the Atlantic — produced the predictable reaction. The creole class did not seek independence because it loved republics; it sought independence because it resented subordination. The republic was the vessel that arrived when the break came.

---

## The Independence Movements

The first sustained independence movement emerged in the second decade of the nineteenth century, drawing on networks among the creole intellectual class and finding its mass base among indigenous and mestizo communities with their own grievances against colonial extraction. The risings of this period were suppressed — Iberia in this decade still had sufficient colonial military capacity to contain what could not be militarily coordinated — but not extinguished. The movements went underground, maintained themselves through correspondence and quiet organisation, and waited.

What changed was not ideology but logistics. In the 1820s, the Iberian Empire faced a strategic demand it could not fully meet: Simón Bolívar's campaigns in northern South America had produced Gran Colombia and were pressing toward the Andean territories. Iberia's decision to concentrate its available colonial military capacity in the south — to hold Peru, to hold the line against Bolívar's southern advance — was rational as a priority calculation. It was catastrophic as a consequence for New Spain.

The plan to use New Spain as a source of northern pressure on Gran Colombia, deploying Mexican garrisons to pin Bolívar's attention, never materialised into meaningful force. The manpower was committed in principle and never arrived in practice; the logistical coordination between Iberia's Caribbean and Pacific colonial chains was less functional under wartime pressure than the planners had assumed. New Spain received orders it could not execute and reinforcements that did not come.

The northern neighbors noticed. Nouvelle Gaule, whose Louisiana territories shared an extensive frontier with Tejas, had a direct interest in the Iberian military presence to its south. Canadia reached the same calculation. Both had maintained formal neutrality on the question of New Spain's status; both now provided what they maintained they were not providing: money, arms, intelligence, and the kind of diplomatic noise that tied up Iberian attention through channels other than the military. The independence movements that had gone underground a decade earlier resurfaced into a different situation: an overstretched Iberian administration, covert external support, and a colonial military that could neither be reinforced nor trusted to suppress what was now coordinated rather than scattered.

---

## The Settlement

By the early 1830s, the independence movement had achieved territorial control over enough of New Spain that re-suppression would require a sustained campaign Iberia was not in a position to mount while also managing Gran Colombia in the south. The Iberian calculation was stated plainly in the diplomatic record: Peru held silver; Mexico held complexity. The choice was made accordingly.

Iberian recognition of Mexican independence was not gracious. It came through intermediaries, with provisions — commercial arrangements, protection of Iberian-connected properties, transit rights — that Mexico accepted and subsequently negotiated away over the following decades as its position strengthened and Iberia's weakened. What the recognition provided was legitimacy and a formal end to the question of reconquest. The colonial presence Iberia retained — Cuba, Puerto Rico, Florida — would be held from the sea, not from a continental base.

## Territory and Federal Structure

The republic that emerged was large and internally diverse in ways that made centralisation implausible from the outset. The arrangement that resulted was federal — more loosely articulated than Gran Colombia's three-constituent model, more structurally complex — because there was no alternative that could hold the territory together.

Mexico's northern states — Tejas, the Californias, Sonora — were geographically distant from the capital and distinct in their economic orientation: ranching, Pacific trade, the extractive industries of the high desert. The central highland states, including Mexico City, concentrated the administrative tradition and the majority of the population. The Gulf and Caribbean coasts were commercially oriented toward maritime trade. The Mesoamerican territories were culturally and linguistically distinct from the north in ways that no federal arrangement could entirely dissolve.

The Mesoamerican integration occurred in stages across the 1830s and 1840s. As Iberia withdrew its presence from the isthmus, the former captaincy-general territories faced a practical question: stand alone or affiliate with a larger entity. Mexico was adjacent, was already constituted, and represented a less alarming prospect than Nouvelle Gaule to the north or an attempt at small-state survival in a world dominated by the League and the still-active Covenant. The affiliations were negotiated rather than imposed; the Mesoamerican states entered as constituent members retaining domestic governance, adding their populations and the strategic value of the isthmus to a federation that now ran from the Rio Bravo to the Darién.

The federal government in Mexico City holds authority over foreign affairs, defence, currency, and external trade. Constituent states retain domestic governance, their own courts, and their own legislatures. The tension between these levels has been the central dynamic of Mexican politics throughout the republic's existence.

---

## The Long Republic

Mexico's nineteenth century was calmer than its independence period but not uneventful. The Catholic Church, which had been an institutional presence throughout the colonial period, retained social authority and remained a factor in Mexican politics that no government could ignore and none found entirely comfortable. The church's relationship to the republic settled into something that satisfied neither side: the church retained its social institutions; the republic retained formal supremacy over civil affairs; and the tension was managed through periodic negotiation and persistent ambiguity that both parties found more bearable than resolution.

The relationship between the federal government and the constituent states produced a different kind of chronic tension. The northern states, economically oriented toward the Northwest Confederation and Nouvelle Gaule and separated from Mexico City by distance that made federal authority feel abstract, developed a political culture of managed autonomy: formally within the federation, practically self-governing in almost everything that mattered locally. Mesoamerica's constituent states developed their own version of this, rooted in cultural distinctiveness rather than geographic isolation.

Neither tendency produced secession. The federation held, not through any single binding myth but through the accumulated institutional investment of a republic that had been operating continuously for longer than anyone could easily remember.

---

## League Membership

Mexico entered the League of Nations in 1907, alongside Ayiti, in the round of admissions that followed the Covenant's 1905 collapse. The timing was long overdue by the estimation of both Mexico and the League's majority: Mexico had been a republic for seven decades, in the League's informal orbit throughout, and its exclusion had been sustained by Iberian pressure rather than any deficiency in its republican credentials.

The admission formalities were brief. The League preferred to treat Mexico as a straightforward new application. Mexico preferred the same.

---

## Position in 2000

Mexico in 2000 is a large, federated, imperfectly but durably republican state. Its federal government manages the external relationships of an entity whose internal diversity would defeat tight centralisation. The north looks toward the Northwest Confederation and Nouvelle Gaule; the Pacific coast orients toward trans-Pacific commerce; the Mesoamerican south has its own orientations, its own languages, its own relationship to what the republic means. Mexico City holds it together through the institutions of a federal republic that has been in continuous operation for a century and a half.

The legacy of its founding is not a military mythology. There is no Mexican Bolívar — no single campaign around which identity crystallises, no decisive moment that the republic returns to for justification. What there is instead is something more diffuse: a republic that arrived through persistence and negotiation rather than decisive victory, which has produced a political culture that is deeply transactional, occasionally frustratingly so, but rarely catastrophically brittle.

Mexico is not the most dramatic polity in the League. It is one of the more durable ones.
