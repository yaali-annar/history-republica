# Kingdom of the Malvinas

**Official name**: Kingdom of the Malvinas (*Reino das Malvinas*)
**Capital**: Puerto Soledad
**Founded**: circa 1907 (Bragança court established at Puerto Soledad following Brazil's republican transition)
**Head of State**: King of the Malvinas (Bragança dynasty)
**League membership**: None

---

## Overview

The Kingdom of the Malvinas is a sub-Antarctic island state occupying the archipelago at the southern extreme of the South American Atlantic coast. It is the only state in the western hemisphere outside the League of Nations, and the only surviving monarchy in South America. It is governed, in nominal terms, by the Bragança dynasty — the former royal house of the United Kingdom of South America — and in practical terms by the financial and legal infrastructure that has made its name synonymous across the League world with offshore accounts, banking secrecy, and the movement of money that cannot be moved anywhere else.

It is most famous for the **Soledad Box**.

---

## The Soledad Box

The Soledad Box — named for Puerto Soledad, the capital and the islands' only significant settlement — is the term used across South America, and increasingly across the League world, for an account held in the Malvinas banking system. The phrase carries its meaning in its name: *soledad* is solitude, isolation, secrecy. A Soledad Box is a container for things you do not wish found.

The Malvinas banking system operates outside League financial regulations, to which the islands are not subject as a non-member state. It offers numbered accounts, nominee ownership structures, and a legal framework in which the identity of an account holder is protected by the islands' own law and cannot be compelled by any foreign jurisdiction. This is not an accident of the islands' poverty or underdevelopment. It is the deliberate policy of a dynasty that had nothing else to sell.

The Bragança court, arriving at Puerto Soledad with its household and its loyalists in the years following Brazil's republican transition, understood its position clearly. Argentina claimed the islands; the League observed with disapproval; neither was willing to use force against a dynasty that was already, in every meaningful sense, finished. The court's survival required making itself useful to people who could protect it from the inconvenience of being removed. The financial secrecy system that developed over the following years was the answer.

By 2000 the Soledad Box is a fixture of South American elite life and an increasingly common feature of League-world financial scandal. Investigations into corruption in the South African Federation, in the UKSA successor states, and occasionally in League member states themselves regularly produce references to accounts in Puerto Soledad that cannot be accessed, holders that cannot be identified, and funds that cannot be repatriated. The League issues periodic statements. The Malvinas does not respond to them.

---

## The Argentine Claim

Argentina has claimed the Malvinas since its 1905 independence declaration, on the grounds that the islands formed part of the Río de la Plata viceroyalty and passed by succession to the Argentine republic. The claim is legally straightforward. It has not been acted upon by force for two reasons: the political cost of dislodging a dynasty that is, formally, no threat to anyone, and the practical reality that a significant portion of the Argentine elite has accounts in Puerto Soledad.

The claim remains active in Argentine domestic politics, where it serves as a reliable source of nationalist sentiment that successive governments have found more useful unresolved than settled.
