# Middle Empire

**Official name**: Middle Empire (*Dulimbai Gurun* in Manchu)
**Predecessor**: Qing Empire
**Core territories**: Manchuria, Mongolia, Tibet, Eastern Turkestan (Xinjiang)
**Status in 2000**: TBD — see open questions

> This document is a stub. The name is settled; constitutional form and post-1905 trajectory remain open.

---

## Overview

The Qing Empire, long the dominant power of East Asia, fractured in the 1850s–1860s when southern China — its wealthiest and most populous region — seceded following a Taiping-equivalent crisis. The south's departure left the Qing as a rump state centred on its Inner Asian territories: Manchuria (the dynasty's ancestral homeland), Mongolia, Tibet, and Xinjiang. What remained was, paradoxically, more genuinely multi-ethnic than the empire at its height — stripped of its Han heartland, the Qing rump was a confederation of Manchu, Mongol, Tibetan, and Turkic peoples bound by dynastic loyalty and Covenant backing.

The Covenant's collapse in 1905 removed Russian support, which had been the rump state's primary external guarantor. Whatever reconstitution followed is the central unsettled question of this entry.

## Settled Canon

### The Southern Secession (1850s–1860s)

The Qing's grip on southern China had been loosening since the early 19th century. Southern commercial elites, drawn into League trading networks through Lanfang, Batam, and Tumasik, increasingly looked to the Straits rather than to Beijing. A Taiping-equivalent crisis in the 1850s–1860s — the specific trigger, figures, and sequence are TBD — provided the breaking point. The south seceded as a predominantly Han republic; the Qing, unable to mount a credible reconquest, was left with its Inner Asian territories and the northern Han plains.

The southern republic accepted the division. It was the initiating party; it had no irredentist claim on territories it had never governed in its own right. By 2000 it is simply called "China" internationally.

### What the Qing Rump Holds

- **Manchuria**: The dynasty's ancestral homeland; the Qing court's core identity
- **Mongolia**: Bound to the Qing through the emperor's simultaneous role as Mongol Khan; Mongolian noble houses integrated into the Qing system
- **Tibet**: Religious-political bond through Tibetan Buddhism; the Qing emperor as patron of the Dalai Lama institution
- **Xinjiang (Eastern Turkestan)**: Uyghur, Kazakh, and other Turkic peoples; strategic buffer between Russia and the south
- **Northern China**: The Han-majority Yellow River basin and Beijing. Whether this remains with the Qing rump or eventually joins the southern republic is unsettled — see open questions

### The Covenant Relationship

Russian military presence in Manchuria made Covenant alignment the path of least resistance for the Qing court. The relationship suited Russia too: a stable Qing buffer kept League influence away from Russia's eastern flank. Korea, as a Qing protectorate, was covered by the same arrangement — Russia backed the Qing, the Qing covered Korea, one chain of patronage rather than two.

The multi-ethnic character of the rump state fit Covenant logic naturally. The Covenant was a bloc of dynastic empires holding diverse peoples together under hereditary authority. The Qing's problem — holding Manchu, Mongol, Tibetan, and Turkic peoples in a single political structure — was structurally similar to Austria-Hungary's.

### After 1905

Japan's defeat of Russia triggered the Covenant's collapse. The Qing, stripped of Russian backing, faced internal pressures that Russian support had suppressed: Mongolian nobles calculating independence, Tibetan religious authorities reassessing the dynastic relationship, Xinjiang in perennial low-level unrest. The reconstitution that followed is the central open question.

## Open Questions

### Name
Settled: **Middle Empire**, from the Manchu *Dulimbai Gurun* — the name the Qing used for themselves in their own language. After losing the Han heartland, the successor state foregrounds the Manchu linguistic tradition rather than the Chinese one. The south takes "China" internationally; the north retakes the Manchu form of the same concept. The name asserts a claim to universal centrality that the territory no longer geographically supports — a deliberate political statement rather than a description. Neither state concedes the other's legitimacy; the League of Nations papers over the tension with careful diplomatic language.

### Imperial or Federal?
Does the Qing dynasty survive the 1905 crisis in some form — a constitutional emperor as ceremonial figurehead, analogous to the Northern Realm's High Guardian — or is the imperial structure abolished entirely in the reconstitution? A constitutional federation with a Qing figurehead would preserve dynastic continuity while distributing actual governance to the constituent peoples. A full abolition would require some other binding principle.

### Northern Han China
Beijing and the Yellow River basin are Han-majority territory that happened to be on the Qing side of the 1850s–1860s division. After 1905, does this territory:
- Remain with the Inner Asian successor state (giving it a significant Han population and administrative base)
- Gravitate toward the southern republic (reuniting Han China under one government)
- Become a contested or transitional zone

### Korea Post-1905
Korea as Qing protectorate loses its protector when the Covenant collapses. Japan is the dominant League-aligned power in the region. Korea's trajectory — League membership, Japanese-brokered transition, attempted independence — is unsettled.

### Lanfang Connection
The Qing tributary relationship with Lanfang, maintained through the whole period of Lanfang's development, becomes complicated after the southern Chinese secession. Lanfang is a Chinese republic that recognises the southern state; does it also maintain formal relations with the Qing rump? The overseas Chinese communities that Lanfang anchored are mostly from southern China — their allegiance after the secession probably runs to the south, not to the Manchu court.
