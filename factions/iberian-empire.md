# The Iberian Empire

**Official name**: Imperio Ibérico / Império Ibérico  
**Capital**: Madrid; Lisbon retained as co-seat of government  
**Founded**: circa 1790  
**Status in 2000**: Dissolved; successor states include the Kingdom of Spain, the Portuguese Republic, and the independent nations formed from former colonial territories

---

## Overview

The Iberian Empire was the product of crisis: a dynastic union activated by revolutionary upheaval in the Americas and metropolitan France, which transformed a pre-existing royal marriage into a formal political merger. It was, for most of its existence, the Covenant's most geographically extensive member — an empire spanning the Iberian peninsula, most of South America, Central America, the Philippines, Timor, and a transcontinental strip of southern Africa — and its only substantial maritime and colonial power. It was also, for most of its existence, chronically overstretched relative to its administrative and financial resources.

The Iberian Empire proposed the Covenant's Colonial Protocol and depended on its partners' gold and settlers to sustain an empire that Iberian population alone could not administer. Its history is largely the history of managing that gap between territorial extent and governing capacity, punctuated by the loss of Gran Colombia to Bolívar's independence campaigns and the eventual unravelling that followed the Covenant's 1905 collapse.

---

## The Founding Union

The dynastic connection that became the Iberian Empire had been in place since 1785, when João, heir to the Portuguese throne, married Carlota Joaquina of Spain, a Bourbon princess. The marriage was conventional dynastic diplomacy, not a project for political union; no one in either court in 1785 expected it to become the seed of an empire.

What changed was 1789. The fall of the French monarchy, the declaration of the French Republic, and the upheaval in the Caribbean produced a crisis that neither Spain nor Portugal could face comfortably alone. Spain's specific wound — the Santo Domingo Affair and its aftermath — pushed the Spanish court toward a partnership it had not previously sought. Portugal, watching the same events from a short distance and confronting the same existential question about its vast colonial empire, recognised that two Iberian monarchies were stronger than one.

The negotiations were rapid. The union was formalised around 1790, placing João and Carlota Joaquina as joint monarchs of a united Iberian crown. The terms were explicit about Portuguese interests: Brazil was to remain administered through Lisbon's traditions rather than Madrid's; Portuguese institutional practices were preserved rather than absorbed into the Spanish model. Portugal had ended the first Iberian Union in 1640 because it felt subordinated. The founders of the second understood that memory and wrote around it.

---

## The Santo Domingo Affair

The wound that defined Iberian politics for a generation was not a military defeat but a public humiliation. Louis XVI, facing bankruptcy and republican rebellion, secretly offered Saint-Domingue — the richest colony in the Caribbean — to Spain in exchange for military intervention to suppress the French republican movement. The offer became public during the revolutionary crisis. It destroyed Louis XVI's remaining political support and ended any prospect of moderate monarchist intervention in France.

For Spain, the public revelation was corrosive in a different way. Spain had been offered a bribe to do the French monarchy's fighting. The offer implied that Spain was purchasable, that its military was for hire, that its Caribbean position was a diplomatic commodity rather than sovereign territory. That the offer became public before Spain could accept or refuse it saved Spain from having been seen to accept — but did not save Spain from having been seen to be offered.

The aftermath compounded the wound. Saint-Domingue declared independence in 1789, abolishing slavery at founding. The new state briefly occupied Spanish Santo Domingo — Spanish territory, attacked by a republic that the old French monarchy had tried to give to Spain as a bribe months earlier. The sequence was complete: humiliation, revelation, attack. The Iberian union that followed was built partly on the shared understanding that this must not happen again.

## The Colonial Empire

The Iberian Empire inherited the accumulated colonial territories of two centuries of Spanish and Portuguese expansion:

**The Americas**: Most of South America, including Brazil, Peru, the Río de la Plata territories, Chile, and the Andean interior. Central America from the Yucatán to Panama. The Caribbean holdings that remained after Saint-Domingue's independence.

**Asia**: The Philippines — Spain's Pacific possession, a substantial colonial administration maintained until independence was declared in the 1905 collapse window. Portuguese Timor, a smaller holding in the eastern Indonesian archipelago.

**Africa**: The pink strip — a transcontinental belt of territory connecting the Atlantic coast of Angola to the Indian Ocean coast of Mozambique. In the European diplomatic competition of the nineteenth century, this connection required sustained effort and Covenant support to achieve and hold against the commercial interests of League members who found an Iberian interior Africa inconvenient. The strip was consolidated and administered with significant Prussian organisational assistance under the Colonial Protocol.

This empire was vast and chronically understaffed. The total population of the Iberian peninsula was insufficient to administer territories of this scale. Colonial governance depended on local intermediaries, religious orders, and the settlers provided through the Covenant's Colonial Protocol. Prussian and Austrian communities in the interior of Brazil and parts of the African strip were not a curiosity but a structural necessity.

---

## Gibraltar

The peninsula's western tip commanded the entrance to the Mediterranean Sea. Gibraltar gave the Iberian Empire leverage that far exceeded what its naval strength alone would have justified — leverage over League commercial traffic, over Mediterranean access, over the terms of every negotiation that touched on trade between the Atlantic and the inland sea.

This leverage was exercised carefully. The Empire's own colonial revenues depended on Mediterranean trade remaining open. The threat of closing the strait was the asset; using it would have damaged Iberia as much as its targets. Gibraltar functioned as a permanent bargaining chip, invoked in every serious negotiation but deployed as an actual blockade almost never.

## The Covenant and the Colonial Protocol

The Iberian Empire joined the Covenant at its founding and proposed its most significant internal institution in response to the greatest colonial crisis of the nineteenth century.

Simón Bolívar's campaigns across the northern tier of South America demonstrated that Iberian resources — soldiers, administrators, naval forces — could not hold an empire of this geographic extent against a sustained independence movement backed, even discretely, by League commercial relationships and moral support. Gran Colombia emerged from the campaigns as a permanent loss: a federal republic of three constituent nations that Iberia could not reconquer.

Iberia's response was to internalise the problem within the Covenant. The Colonial Protocol committed Prussia and Austria to supplying settlers, soldiers, and administrative personnel for Iberian colonial territories in exchange for a share of colonial revenues. Russia contributed financial capital from Siberian gold and Ural metals. The arrangement was not charity; each Covenant partner calculated that an Iberia whose colonial revenues collapsed would be a weaker ally and a less stable anchor for the Covenant's Atlantic and Mediterranean position.

The Protocol produced results. The southern colonies — Peru, Chile, the Río de la Plata — held through the nineteenth century. Brazil remained within the empire longer than might otherwise have been expected. The African pink strip was consolidated under Prussian organisational assistance.

---

## Gran Colombia and the Northern Loss

The permanent failure of the Covenant's reconquest effort against Gran Colombia defined the limits of what the Colonial Protocol could achieve. Bolívar's federation had time, terrain, and a cause that outlasted the financial and military effort directed against it. The sustained external pressure intended to destroy it forged it instead: each failed reconquest attempt deepened the shared identity of the three constituent nations and made dissolution of the federation less, not more, likely.

Bolívar's advance southward was halted before it reached the Andean interior. The line that emerged — Gran Colombia's southern border roughly following the northern edge of the Peruvian territories — became a permanent geopolitical boundary. North of it was a League-aligned republic; south of it was Iberian colonial territory sustained by Covenant support.

The loss was permanent and known to be so. Iberian political life thereafter was marked by a recurring argument about what Gran Colombia represented: a recoverable territory or a demonstration of the empire's limits. The empire's practical answer, never officially stated, was the latter.

---

## Decline and Dissolution

The Covenant's collapse in 1905 removed the financial and human infrastructure on which the empire depended. Prussian gold and Austrian settlers had been structural; without them, the administrative gap that had always existed became critical. The Philippines declared independence in this window. The African territories began the negotiations that would define the successor states of the following decades.

What remained of the Iberian Empire after 1905 was a diminished metropolitan state and a colonial situation in managed retreat. The dynastic union itself did not survive the loss of common purpose; Spain and Portugal separated constitutionally in the early twentieth century, each following its own path toward what came after.
