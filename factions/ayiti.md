# Federal Republic of Ayiti

**Official name**: Federal Republic of Ayiti  
**Common name**: SD; informally "the SDs" among League members  
**Capital**: Maguana (Federal District)  
**Constituent republics**: Republic of Saint-Domingue (west); Republic of Santo Domingo (east)  
**Official languages**: French (Saint-Domingue); Spanish (Santo Domingo); both co-official at federal level  
**Founded**: 1823  
**League membership**: League of Nations, 1907  

---

## Overview

The Federal Republic of Ayiti occupies the island of Hispaniola in the Caribbean Sea. It is a union of two sovereign republics — Saint-Domingue to the west, Santo Domingo to the east — sharing defence, foreign policy, and currency, each retaining its own parliament, courts, and domestic governance. In structure it resembles the United Nations of Great Britain and Ireland more than any unitary state, though the comparison is rarely made outside academic circles; Ayitians tend to regard the arrangement as their own invention and are not wrong to do so.

Among League members the federation is commonly called SD — a shorthand that predates the federation by decades, which the Ayitians themselves use. "Saint-Domingue Espagne" and "Santo Domingo Français" serve as qualifiers when disambiguation is necessary; the island's own inhabitants use them sparingly.

Ayiti is the oldest republic in the Caribbean and among the oldest in the Atlantic world. Its founding abolished slavery — not reformed it, not restricted it, but abolished it outright, at a moment when the practice remained entrenched across most of the hemisphere. The republic born from that act has had two centuries to develop what a free society on a Caribbean island becomes: a trading hub of considerable sophistication and, since the mid-nineteenth century, the principal destination for African students seeking a university education outside the continent.

## The Revolution of 1789

France's revolution did not produce Saint-Domingue's. It merely provided the conditions in which Saint-Domingue could produce its own.

When Paris convulsed in the summer of 1789, the colonial administration in Saint-Domingue was already stretched thin. The plantation economy had long concentrated wealth across a deeply stratified society: grand blancs, petit blancs, affranchis — free people of colour — and the enslaved majority who constituted the colony's labour and its most urgent political fact. The question of what the Rights of Man meant in a colony where most people were property had no comfortable answer, and the colonial administration's efforts to avoid it had produced years of tension.

The revolution in France did not answer the question. It removed the authority that might have suppressed what followed. By the time a new French republican government was sufficiently organized to send instructions to Saint-Domingue, it received word instead: the island had already resolved its own affairs.

What had occurred was not a single event. The enslaved majority rose in August 1789, in numbers that overwhelmed every organized resistance. The colonial militia collapsed within weeks. The grand blanc planter class, whose interests had always been colonial rather than metropolitan, faced a choice between flight and accommodation; most chose flight. The affranchis — many of them educated, propertied, and politically organized — moved into the space that opened and provided the leadership around which the revolution consolidated.

François-Dominique Toussaint, a freed man of uncommon ability, emerged as the organizing mind of that consolidation. By the end of 1789 he held effective military and political control. The founding document of the Republic of Saint-Domingue, promulgated in January 1790, declared the island sovereign, abolished slavery in all its forms, and established a republican government with Toussaint as Governor.

The new French republic, receiving this news while managing its own upheaval, concluded it had no practical alternative but to recognize what had already happened. The recognition was swift but not gracious.

## The Santo Domingo Affair

The founding document of the Republic of Saint-Domingue described the island. Both halves of it.

This was a statement of aspiration rather than fact, and most of the republic's leadership understood as much. The eastern portion of Hispaniola — Santo Domingo — remained a Spanish colony: Catholic, Spanish-speaking, pastorally oriented, with a smaller enslaved population than the western plantation economy had required, and a substantial free Black and mixed-race population integrated into civic life in ways the French plantation colony had never permitted. It was not Saint-Domingue. But it was on the same island, and slavery was present.

Toussaint resolved the question in the way that had characterized his military career: directly. In 1791, Saint-Domingue forces crossed the border, occupied Santo Domingo's principal settlements, and killed the Spanish Governor.

The Maritime League learned of this from the Spanish envoy who arrived at The Hague in a state of controlled fury. The League's position was stated carefully: Saint-Domingue was not a member, and the League could not be held responsible for the actions of non-member states. Spain then addressed the same demand to France. The French diplomat's reply became one of the more quoted exchanges in Caribbean diplomatic history: *When we convened our first Assembly, they had already declared themselves the Republic of Saint-Domingue.* France had not authorized the action. France did not control the actor.

Spain departed The Hague with no territorial redress, no reparation, and no party formally liable for its Governor's death. The League, which had privately conveyed to Toussaint that the operation was inadvisable — advice he had not sought — worked through back channels to frame the Governor's death as collateral rather than deliberate. Spain did not believe this characterization but found it useful to accept, as the alternative was to be seen as unable to extract even an apology.

The League then made clear to Toussaint, through channels no one would acknowledge, what the practical arrangements were. Saint-Domingue was not a League member. It would not become one in the near term. It would also not face Spain alone.

In Santo Domingo itself, the occupation forced a question the independence movement had been preparing to answer for some time. That movement was genuinely cross-class and cross-racial — criollo ranchers, free Black families, a small educated elite that had followed the foundings of the Union of States and Nouvelle Gaule with close attention and drawn their own conclusions. The decades of contact with League commerce had done their quiet work. What the movement had not wanted was liberation by their neighbor. When Spain formally demanded restoration of its territory and League pressure produced Toussaint's withdrawal, the answer from Santo Domingo was unambiguous: they would not return to Spain, and they would not become Saint-Domingue.

Juan Sánchez Ramírez — a wealthy criollo rancher who had spent years as a loyalist, petitioning Spain for relief and receiving none — led the declaration of the Republic of Santo Domingo in 1792. He had not converted to republican ideology; he had concluded that Spain had effectively abandoned the colony by failing to hold it, and that what remained was to govern what was left. The ideological character of the new republic owed more to the movement's other factions than to Sánchez Ramírez himself. He provided legitimacy; they provided conviction. Spain was left with nothing.

A Spanish naval force arrived off Santo Domingo's coast the following year — sufficient in size to demonstrate that Madrid had responded, insufficient to reconquer a territory whose population wanted none of it. The expedition's commander assessed what he found: a population offering no cooperation, Saint-Domingue forces visibly repositioning to the west, and supply lines that grew more tenuous the longer the ships remained at anchor. He dispatched his assessment to Madrid and withdrew. Spain drew its conclusions about what it could and could not recover, and redirected its Caribbean attention accordingly. Garrisons in Cuba and Puerto Rico were reinforced; colonial assemblies faced tighter restrictions; the lesson Spain took from Hispaniola was about what it could not afford to lose next.

The republic that answered for France at The Hague was two years old. The kingdom that had owned Saint-Domingue no longer existed. Whatever claim France might have pressed, it had no government left that could press it.

## Toussaint's Later Years

The Santo Domingo affair damaged Toussaint's political standing in ways that covert League support could not repair. He had acted without consulting the leadership he had built around him; he had killed a foreign official and handed the island's enemies a grievance; and his withdrawal — however carefully managed — read as a reversal.

The League had, prior to the operation, raised the question of the Governor-for-Life provision in Saint-Domingue's founding document. The advice was that republican governance implied succession mechanisms that did not depend on any single person's willingness to leave. Toussaint had not formally responded. After 1792, the internal pressure to act on it arrived without further prompting from anyone outside.

He accepted a fixed term with an age ceiling in 1796 — a provision modelled loosely on the English constitutional precedent — and governed under it until the term concluded. He did not seek another. He spent his remaining years as a senior figure without office, present at state occasions, consulted by successors who understood what deference was owed and paid it accordingly. He died in 1817. His reputation in Ayiti is neither the unambiguous triumph of a founding myth nor the tragedy it might have been. He built the republic, and the one time he moved without counsel the consequences took a generation to resolve. Ayitian civic education considers this an instructive combination.

## The Shared Island

The two republics spent the following decades doing what neighboring small states on the same island inevitably do: they traded, intermarried, argued, and gradually found the border less relevant to daily life than the administrative lines suggested. The geography enforced proximity. The abolition of slavery in both republics from their foundings removed the economic structure that might have required the border to be a barrier.

By the early nineteenth century, inhabitants of both sides were using SD as shorthand for the island entire. Outsiders requiring precision received "Saint-Domingue Espagne" from the French-speaking side and "Santo Domingo Français" from the Spanish-speaking side — each naming the other in their own language, which is characteristic of how people talk about neighbors when they are intimate enough to find formal names unnecessary.

## The Federation

The Federal Republic of Ayiti was constituted in 1823.

The impetus was partly internal — two republics whose citizens had been treating the border as notional for a generation — and partly external. Both had been in informal League orbit for three decades without membership, and the League had indicated through familiar back channels that a unified applicant was more tractable than two separate ones with a complicated shared history. The constitution of 1823 followed the British model more than either republic's founding document had followed anything: two sovereign entities sharing defence, foreign policy, and currency, each retaining its own parliament, courts, and domestic law.

The federal capital was established at Maguana, a new city built in the interior near the old border, on the site of a Taíno cacicazgo of the same name. The choice was deliberate — neither French-named nor Spanish-named, belonging to neither side's colonial heritage, marking that the union was naming itself after what the island was before either republic arrived.

The federal head of government is the Governor of the Federal Republic, elected by the federal assembly for a fixed term. Each constituent republic elects its own Governor for domestic affairs. The division of authority between the two levels has not always been clean, but it has held.

## League Membership

The Federal Republic of Ayiti applied for League of Nations membership in 1906, following the collapse of the Covenant after Russia's defeat by Japan. The Iberian Empire, whose founding grievance had involved Santo Domingo and whose influence had kept Ayiti outside the League for over a century, was in no position to object. Ayiti was admitted alongside Mexico, whose long path from Iberian colony to independent republic had reached a similar conclusion through different circumstances.

The admission formalities were conducted with some ceremony. The League, which had been providing covert support to Saint-Domingue since 1791, preferred to treat the membership as a straightforward new application rather than the conclusion of a long prior relationship. Ayiti was content to let it be treated that way.

## Education and Trade

Ayiti's position as the Atlantic world's first Black republic — founded on abolition, stable within a generation, and accumulating the institutions that stability permits — made it the natural destination for African students from the mid-nineteenth century onward. The University of Saint-Domingue, founded in 1834, expanded its intake of students from West and Central Africa as the century progressed. By 1880 the island hosted the largest concentration of African academics outside the continent itself.

The pattern has persisted. An Ayitian degree carries specific weight in African professional contexts that has little to do with its rank in League university tables and much to do with what that institution has meant for two centuries. The university produces graduates who return to Lagos, Accra, Dakar, and Zanzibar carrying credentials and a particular set of assumptions about what a republic can be and who it is for.

The trade function developed alongside the educational one and was reinforced by it. Port-au-Prince and Santo Domingo city are established commercial ports; Maguana has developed its own trade in services. The federal republic's position at the intersection of Atlantic, Caribbean, and Gulf commercial routes, combined with two centuries of republican stability, has produced the institutional density — banks, insurers, brokers, legal firms — that such a position rewards.
