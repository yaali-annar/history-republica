## Overview

The Northwest Confederation is a confederation of indigenous nations, settler communities, and former colonial territories occupying the Pacific coast and interior west of North America — bounded to the east by Nouvelle Gaule and Canadia, to the south by Mexico, and extending north through former Russian Alaska to the Arctic. It is among the more unusual polities in the League of Nations: constituted by genuine plurality, its external boundaries are solid and internationally recognised while its internal jurisdictions are deliberately unresolved.

The Confederation is commonly known in League contexts by its geographic designation. Its formal name in the majority administrative language of the central council does not translate cleanly into English or French, and League documents render it variously, none satisfactorily.

---

## Origins: The Territory

The interior west of the North American continent remained, through the first decades of the nineteenth century, formally unclaimed by any recognised republic or empire while being home to numerous indigenous nations whose territorial relationships with one another were complex, ancient, and not organised around the fixed boundary concepts that European diplomatic practice expected.

The question of what this territory would become acquired urgency as its neighbours consolidated. To the south, Mexico's independence in the 1830s replaced an Iberian military presence with a republican one but left the Pacific Northwest beyond Mexico's settled northern territories effectively unaddressed. To the north, Russian Alaska pressed down from the Bering coast — the Covenant's only presence in the Americas, a strategic fact that Canadia and Nouvelle Gaule, both in the League's orbit, found it useful to think about carefully.

The Northwest Territory, as League diplomatic records came to call it, was the result of that thinking. Canadia and Nouvelle Gaule, through a combination of commercial arrangements, treaty recognition, and material support that neither government formally acknowledged, fostered a framework in which the indigenous nations of the Pacific Northwest could present a recognised territorial face to the outside world without requiring the imposition of European governance models. The indigenous council structures that existed were validated and provisioned; settler communities that had arrived through the fur trade and Pacific commerce were given standing within those structures rather than placed above them. The League extended informal recognition to the Territory as a distinct entity — neither a colony nor a republic in the standard form — while managing its relationships with neighbouring states.

The Territory served its strategic purpose. Russian Alaska did not expand south. Claims to the Pacific coast above Mexico's settled territories found no opening. The Territory's external coherence — whatever its internal complexity — was a working fact.

## The Covenant Period

Through the nineteenth century, the Northwest Territory coexisted with Russian Alaska across what both sides treated as a de facto boundary. The Covenant's colonial interests in the Americas were primarily Iberian — its Caribbean and South American holdings — and Russia's Alaskan presence was strategic and extractive rather than economically developed. Occasional tensions over the boundary produced League-mediated clarifications that held without formal demarcation.

The Territory in this period was self-governing in the sense that mattered: no external power administered it. The central council — drawn from indigenous nations across the territory — handled external communications, managed the relationships with Canadia and Nouvelle Gaule that formed its material base, and arbitrated the disputes that reached its level. Most disputes did not reach its level. Most governance happened within individual nations on terms those nations had developed for themselves, in languages the central council did not require its members to abandon.

## Alaska and the Confederation

The Covenant's collapse in 1905, following Russia's defeat by Japan, changed the Territory's position. Russia, managing acute internal pressures and the restructuring of its European arrangements, found Alaska increasingly difficult to sustain at cost. Alaska's population — Russian settlers concentrated around trading posts and coastal communities, and indigenous Alaskan peoples whose relationship to Russian colonial authority had always been more nominal than functional — had long since developed its own institutions for managing the distance between Russian governance in theory and Russian capacity in practice.

The transfer was arranged rather than dramatic. League mediation provided the framework; Russia's agreement was secured as part of broader post-Covenant normalization; Alaska's communities, given formal voice in the process, chose affiliation with the Territory over the alternatives. The agreements took several years to conclude and several more to implement. What they produced was a Territory that now ran to the Arctic, incorporating peoples and communities whose relationship to the rest of the Territory had previously been defined by a boundary rather than any shared institution.

The formal constitution of the Confederation followed from this expansion. A Territory of informal arrangement could manage a certain scale; an entity of this geographic reach required recognised institutions. The central council was formalised, its composition and authorities specified in writing for the first time, and the Confederation was presented to the League as a full member applicant. It was admitted in 1907, alongside Mexico and Ayiti, in the round of accessions that the Covenant's collapse had made available.

## Structure and Internal Borders

The Confederation's external boundaries are among the more precisely demarcated in the Americas, a product of the formal processes that accompanied Alaska's absorption and the League membership negotiations that followed. Its internal jurisdictions are a different matter entirely.

The constituent entities of the Confederation — indigenous nations, settler districts, the former Russian colonial communities of Alaska — hold their territories on terms established through council agreements, historical occupation, and the accumulated outcomes of generations of inter-nation negotiation. These terms do not map onto surveyed lines. A river valley may be recognised as a fishing territory by three nations simultaneously, each with legitimate basis for the claim and none prepared to surrender it. A settler town established within the territory of an indigenous nation is governed under arrangements specific to its founding that do not extend to the next settlement. Former Russian Alaska has its own internal layering: indigenous Alaskan nations whose territorial relationships with one another substantially predate Russian arrival, and Russian settler communities whose standing in the Confederation was negotiated as a specific category and whose legal position differs from that of older settler communities in the south.

The Confederation's central government does not adjudicate these questions, because no constituent entity has proposed granting it that authority, and the proposal would not survive a council vote if made. What the central government does instead is provide a forum in which disputes that constituent entities cannot resolve bilaterally can be mediated — with outcomes that depend on the parties' willingness to accept them rather than on enforcement.

In practice this works better than external observers expect. The internal disputes of the Northwest Confederation are chronic and occasionally heated. They are rarely violent. The cost of violence is too high for parties who share the council chamber and who know that the alternative to mediation is an unresolved dispute with a neighbour they cannot leave.

## External Relations

The Confederation's most significant external relationships are with its immediate neighbours: Canadia and Nouvelle Gaule to the east, Mexico to the south, and the Russia Federation to the north across the Arctic approaches.

The relationship with Canadia and Nouvelle Gaule has evolved from dependence to partnership. The Confederation no longer requires external material support to function; it trades with its founding sponsors on terms it negotiates rather than accepts. What remains is a diplomatic intimacy — the Confederation's delegates in League forums tend to consult Canadia and Nouvelle Gaule before major votes — that reflects history without reproducing the old dependency.

The relationship with the Russia Federation is managed and watchful. Russia's League membership has normalised relations at the institutional level; the historical memory of Russian Alaska, and the process by which it was absorbed, means the Confederation follows developments in the Russian north with attention that neighbourly courtesy alone would not produce.

## The Confederation in 2000

The Northwest Confederation in 2000 is regularly cited in League political theory debates about what republican governance requires and whether its standard forms exhaust the possibilities. It is not a republic in the standard sense — its central institutions are not organised around individual citizen representation in the form the League's founding model assumes — but it is not a monarchy, not a traditional confederation in the historical European sense, and not easily slotted into any other category League taxonomy provides.

What it is, its own administrators would say, is a working arrangement that has lasted for generations and produced less violence and more welfare for its constituent peoples than the alternatives on offer in the 1820s. That claim is broadly accepted by its neighbours.

What it is not, they would also say, is finished. The internal border questions that the Confederation has declined to resolve have not resolved themselves. They are simply chronic rather than acute — a condition the Confederation has learned to manage and has not yet found a reason to change.
