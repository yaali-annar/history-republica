# Gran Colombia

**Official name**: Republic of Gran Colombia  
**Capital**: Bogotá  
**Constituted**: circa 1820s  
**Constituent Republics**: Venezuela, New Granada, Ecuador  
**League of Nations**: Full member

---

## Overview

Gran Colombia is a federal republic occupying the northern tier of South America — the territories of present-day Venezuela, Colombia, and Ecuador, united under a shared federal government since the independence campaigns of the 1820s. It is the most direct legacy of Simón Bolívar's military and political project: a continental union that, against the expectations of most observers at its founding and most of its enemies thereafter, held together.

The federation survived where Bolívar's vision was expected to fail because the force trying to destroy it — the Iberian Empire backed by the full resources of the Covenant — provided what internal politics alone might not have: a common enemy sustained long enough to make federal identity feel real. By the time the Iberian reconquest effort was exhausted, Gran Colombia had existed long enough that its constituent nations had invested too much in the union to dissolve it.

---

## The Constituent Republics

Gran Colombia's federal structure distributes domestic authority to its three constituent republics while sharing foreign policy, defence, and currency at the federal level.

**Venezuela** occupies the northern coast and the Llanos interior, with Caracas as its capital. Its Caribbean-facing geography made it the first territory where Bolívar's campaigns achieved critical momentum, and Venezuelan military contributions to the independence wars were disproportionate to its population. Venezuela is the republic most associated with the founding generation and most invested in the federal mythology.

**New Granada** occupies the Andean interior and both the Pacific and Atlantic coasts between Venezuela and Ecuador. Bogotá, in the highland interior, is both the capital of New Granada and the seat of Gran Colombia's federal government — a choice that reflects New Granada's central position more than any sentimental primacy. New Granada is the most populous constituent republic and the administrative centre of the federation.

**Ecuador** occupies the southern Andean territories and the Pacific coast, with Quito as its capital. Ecuador was the last of the three territories to be secured in the independence campaigns and the most exposed to Iberian pressure from the south, where the Peruvian colonial territories remained under Iberian control throughout the nineteenth century. Ecuador's relationship with the federation has periodically been the most complicated — a peripheral position and a southern border that remained contested long after independence was consolidated elsewhere.

## Independence and Foundation

The campaigns that produced Gran Colombia began in the second decade of the nineteenth century, when Simón Bolívar organised the independence movements of northern South America into a coordinated military and political project. The goal from the outset was not merely independence from Iberia but a union of the liberated territories: Bolívar understood that fragmented successor states would be individually vulnerable to reconquest in ways that a federation would not.

The federation was constituted as the military campaigns were still ongoing. The federal structure — three constituent republics, shared federal institutions, a capital at Bogotá — was a political choice made under pressure, designed to accommodate the regional differences that might otherwise have fragmented the movement before independence was secured.

League support during this period was real but carefully managed. The Maritime League could not formally admit Gran Colombia as a member while the independence campaigns were still contested without provoking direct Covenant retaliation. What it provided was commercial relationship, diplomatic pressure on Iberia through other channels, and the assurance that League members would not permit material Covenant intervention to tip the balance. Gran Colombia fought its wars largely with its own forces; it did not fight them entirely alone.

## The Reconquest Wars

The Iberian Empire, backed by the Colonial Protocol commitments of its Covenant partners, mounted sustained reconquest efforts across the 1820s and 1830s. The campaigns were well-resourced — Prussian military advisors, Russian gold, Iberian naval forces — and initially alarming to the federation's survival.

They failed for a compound of reasons. The terrain favoured defenders. Supply lines from Iberia and Covenant Europe were long. The federation's forces had learned the landscape in ways that expeditionary forces never fully matched. And there was the political dynamic the Covenant had not anticipated: each year the reconquest continued was a year in which Grancolombians — Venezuelans, New Granadans, Ecuadorans — experienced themselves as fighting the same war against the same enemy. The Covenant's persistence built the national identity it was trying to destroy.

Bolívar's attempt to extend the campaigns southward into Peru and the Andean interior was halted by a combination of Iberian defensive success and the logistical limits of campaigning across the mountain barrier. The line that emerged — Gran Colombia's southern border roughly following the northern edge of the Peruvian territories — became permanent. South of it, the Iberian Empire held. North of it, it never recovered.

## Federal Structure

Gran Colombia's constitution distributes authority on principles that its founders derived partly from the Union of States model and partly from the specific conditions of the independence campaigns.

Each constituent republic holds full domestic authority: its own parliament, courts, tax administration, and internal governance. The federal government in Bogotá holds authority over foreign policy, defence, currency, and inter-republic trade. No constituent republic may conduct its own foreign policy or conclude its own treaties.

The federal legislature draws representatives from all three constituent republics. Major federal decisions require supermajorities that prevent any single republic from dominating the others — a provision inserted specifically to address Venezuela's early numerical and military preponderance and Ecuador's persistent concern that its peripheral position made it dispensable to the other two.

## League Membership and World Position

Gran Colombia was in the Maritime League's orbit from independence, though formal membership came later — the sustained Iberian threat made immediate League admission a diplomatic complication the League managed carefully. Full membership in the League of Nations followed the Covenant's 1905 collapse, which removed the Iberian Empire as an active threat and allowed formalisation of a relationship that had existed in practice for generations.

In 2000, Gran Colombia is a mid-sized federal republic whose political culture is shaped by the founding experience: a war of survival against a great power backed by the world's dominant monarchist bloc, won by holding together when dissolution would have been easier. The federation is periodically tense — the three constituent republics have divergent economic interests and persistent regional identities — but dissolution has not been seriously proposed in the twentieth century. The founding wars are recent enough in political memory that no political movement has found it useful to discover what it would mean to face a hostile continent as three separate small nations.
