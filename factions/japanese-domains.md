# Japanese Domains

**Official name**: Japanese Domains (日本諸藩, *Nihon Shōhan*)
**Short form**: Japan
**Status**: League of Nations member
**Capital (political)**: Edo
**Capital (religious/cultural)**: Kyoto
**Head of government**: Shogun (elected)
**Head of state (ceremonial/religious)**: Emperor (*Tennō*)

---

## Overview

The Japanese Domains are a confederation of feudal domains across the Japanese archipelago, governed through an elected Shogun and a Council of Lords that together constitute the world's most unusual marriage of feudal structure and constitutional principle. The Emperor, resident in Kyoto, holds no political authority; his role is religious — High Priest of the Shinto tradition, divine ancestor of the Japanese people, the ritual embodiment of national continuity. The domains that compose the confederation retain genuine autonomy over their own governance, contributing military capacity and revenue to the centre while administering their own affairs.

Japan's defeat of Russia in 1905 was the event that triggered the Covenant's collapse — the moment that reshaped the world order more decisively than any other in the twentieth century. It was the product of a constitutional system that had spent twenty years building military capacity from a distributed base, League partnerships that provided modern shipbuilding and strategic intelligence, and a political structure unified enough to act but flexible enough to survive.

## History

### Before the Settlement

For two centuries before the constitutional settlement, Japan was governed by the Tokugawa Shogunate — a hereditary military government in Edo that dominated the daimyo by obliging lords to spend alternate years in the capital as a form of political control. The Emperor in Kyoto was politically irrelevant, performing Shinto rituals while the Shogunate administered the country. The arrangement was stable but brittle: the Shogunate's authority rested on Tokugawa family dominance, not on any constitutional foundation.

By the mid-19th century, the external environment was changing in ways the Tokugawa system was poorly designed to handle. Russia was expanding into Manchuria. The Qing Empire, sheltering under Covenant recognition, held Korea as a protectorate. League commercial networks were pressing at Japan's edges. The question facing Japan was not whether to engage with the outside world but how — and who would make that decision.

### The Lords' War (c. 1870s)

The crisis broke along the fault line that had always run through Tokugawa Japan: the tension between centralising authority and the daimyo's desire for genuine autonomy. Two factions formed around incompatible answers to the question of how Japan should face the Covenant threat.

The **Imperial faction** argued for restoring full authority to the Emperor — a centralised state with the court at its apex, unified command, no daimyo interference. Their model looked, in structural terms, like the Covenant's monarchist states. Some among them were in contact with Covenant sympathisers who saw an absolutist Japan as a potential partner against the League's Pacific presence.

The **Domain faction** argued the opposite: the daimyo domains represented distributed capacity the imperial court alone could not match. Let each domain modernise at its own pace, contribute its forces to a common defence, and elect a head of government accountable to the collective. The League connection made this argument strategically credible — a constitutional Japan could draw on League shipyards, intelligence, and commercial networks. An absolutist Japan would have none of that.

The Domain faction won. The settlement that ended the conflict drew explicitly on the Northern Realm model; Japanese reformers had been in correspondence with Stockholm for years, and the High Guardian had received a Japanese constitutional delegation during the conflict itself. The legitimacy that implied — a League-aligned constitutional state recognising Japan's internal settlement — was not incidental to the outcome.

### The Constitutional Settlement

The settlement produced three interlocking institutions.

The **Emperor** retained everything the Imperial faction had claimed for him — divine ancestry, ritual authority, the Shinto high priesthood, the reverence of the Japanese people — and was stripped of everything political. Resident in Kyoto, the Emperor opens Council sessions ceremonially, performs the ritual calendar, and blesses decisions of state. He does not make them. The constitutional documents are precise on this point: the Emperor reigns; the Council governs.

The **Council of Lords** (*Ryōshu-kai*) is the primary legislative body. Major domains hold individual seats; smaller domains are grouped into regional constituencies. The Council votes on legislation, approves treaties and declarations of war, and — the settlement's decisive provision — **elects the Shogun** for a fixed term.

The **Shogun**, recast from hereditary warlord to elected head of government, holds executive authority: foreign policy, national defence, the central treasury. The office is no longer Tokugawa property. Any domain lord meeting the Council's eligibility criteria may stand; any sitting Shogun may be removed by a supermajority vote. The Shogun governs from Edo. The Emperor presides over Kyoto.

Each domain retains authority over its own affairs — local law, taxation, and its own samurai forces, which contribute to the national military under Shogun command during external conflicts.

### Modernisation and the Ryukyu Question

The two decades between the settlement and 1905 were Japan's period of constitutional modernisation. Unlike a centralised model, this modernisation was distributed: each domain modernised at its own pace, drawing on League commercial access, Northern Realm constitutional advisors, and the competitive pressure of watching neighbouring domains develop faster.

The samurai class was not abolished. It was transformed from within — administrators who also understood rifles, domain governors who also understood maritime economics, military officers trained alongside League fleets. The samurai identity persisted as a professional culture rather than a legal status.

The southern Chinese secession of the 1850s–1860s created an unexpected opening for Ryukyu, the island kingdom Satsuma domain had held as a tributary since 1609. Ryukyu had long maintained a dual tributary relationship — paying tribute to both the Qing and to Satsuma — a balancing act that required careful management of two overlords simultaneously. When southern China broke from the Qing, Ryukyu's Chinese tributary relationship became ambiguous: the Qing rump in the north was Covenant-aligned, geographically remote, and increasingly preoccupied with its own reconstitution. Satsuma domain formalised what geography had made practical, issuing a guarantee of protection that effectively released Ryukyu from the obligation to maintain the northern tributary relationship. The Shō dynasty retained its throne, its court, and its governance; it now answered to one master instead of two.

### 1905 and Ryukyu's Independence

Russia's expansion into Manchuria had been the original threat that triggered the Lords' War. By 1905 it became the occasion for Japan to demonstrate what the constitutional settlement had built.

League shipyard access, two decades of naval training alongside League fleets, and a constitutional structure that had spent a generation integrating domain military capacity into a coherent national force produced something Russia's commanders underestimated. Russia was projecting power across thousands of miles of steppe at the end of a single railway line.

Japan won.

Russia's defeat destabilised the Covenant from within — the humiliation of its largest military power exposed the Covenant's foundational assumption that its combined weight was invincible. The cascade that followed across Austria, Prussia, and Iberia was the Covenant's structural failure becoming visible all at once.

The regional reshaping that followed resolved Ryukyu's remaining ambiguity. With the Middle Empire reconstituting without Russian support and the regional order redrawn, Satsuma domain released its protectorate claim. The Shō dynasty became the head of a fully independent Kingdom of Ryukyu — a League member in its own right, small but sovereign, its independence underwritten by Japan's regional weight without being absorbed into Japan's constitutional structure. The arrangement suited both parties: Japan gained a friendly independent neighbour rather than an awkward semi-possession; Ryukyu gained the formal sovereignty its dynasty had always nominally held.

## Japan in 2000

The Japanese Domains in 2000 are a mature constitutional confederation of several dozen domains, their boundaries somewhat evolved from the Edo period through inheritance, consolidation, and constitutional adjustment. The major domains — Satsuma, Choshu, Kaga, Sendai, and others — retain their historical identities and disproportionate weight in the Council of Lords. The Shogun serves an eight-year term, re-electable once.

Edo is one of the world's great cities — political and commercial capital of a confederation that has been industrialising for a century and a half. Kyoto remains the cultural and religious capital, seat of the Emperor's court, destination of pilgrimage and ceremony. The two cities define two aspects of Japanese identity that the constitution deliberately separated.

The samurai-descended class has professionalised across generations into a recognisable administrative culture: competent, formal, proud of continuity, sometimes frustrating to outsiders who want decisions made faster than domain consultation allows.

The persistent domestic tension is between the major domain bloc — whose Council weight reflects Edo-period prestige more than current population — and reformers who argue that a confederation of millions should allocate seats by population rather than domain history. This argument has been ongoing for a century and shows no sign of resolution.

The Kingdom of Ryukyu, independent since 1905, maintains close ties with Japan — treaty of friendship, shared defence arrangements, League cooperation — without being part of Japan's constitutional structure. The Shō dynasty's current head is the forty-third monarch of a line that predates Japanese involvement in the islands by two centuries.

## Position in the World

Japan's 1905 victory over Russia is the hinge event of modern world history. Everything that followed — the Covenant's collapse, the Philippines' liberation, the Middle Empire's reconstitution, the League of Nations replacing the Maritime League — flows from that outcome. Japan is aware of this. The confederation's diplomatic culture carries a quiet confidence that comes from having been proven right about a very large bet.

Its relationship with the League's founding republics is strong but not uncomplicated. English and Dutch commercial interests in Japanese waters predated the constitutional settlement and will outlast any particular political arrangement. Japan's naval tradition, built simultaneously on League shipbuilding and domain military culture, is one of the League's significant assets in the Pacific.

Korea remains the unresolved question. Japan is the dominant power in the region; Korea lost its Qing protector in 1905 and has been navigating the new order ever since. The Domains have no formal claim on Korea and no appetite for colonial administration — but absence of formal claim and absence of influence are not the same thing, and everyone in Seoul understands the difference.

The Emperor, in Kyoto, performs the seasonal rites. The Shogun, in Edo, receives ambassadors. The Council of Lords deliberates. The domains govern themselves. The arrangement, which the Imperial faction once called an insult to Japanese civilisation, has outlasted every empire that called it weak.
