# The Straits Federal District

**Official name**: Straits Federal District (*Daerah Persekutuan Selat*)
**Status**: Federal capital territory of the Nusantara Federation
**Constituted**: c. 1850 (from lease transfers)
**Territories**: Tumasik (northern) and Batam (southern)

---

## Overview

The Straits Federal District is the capital territory of the Nusantara Federation, occupying two small territories at the eastern entrance to the Malacca Straits: **Tumasik** at the southern tip of the Malay Peninsula, and **Batam** in the Riau Archipelago across the water to the south. Between them they sit astride one of the world's great maritime chokepoints.

The District's history is inseparable from the two Western republics who built it. For nearly two centuries before its constitution as federal territory, Tumasik was an English trading post and Batam a Dutch one — leased from the Sultanate of Johor, developed into commercial hubs, and eventually surrendered to become the capital of a federation neither republic had anticipated when they first raised their flags on the strait.

---

## History

### Batam and the Portuguese Expulsion, 1641

The Dutch Republic's position at Batam grew from the joint operation that ended Portuguese control of Malacca. In 1641, the VOC and the Sultanate of Johor combined forces to expel the Portuguese — Johor recovering a degree of sovereignty over the Straits it had lost when Malacca fell in 1511, and the Dutch securing a commercial position at the strait's eastern entrance. The Batam lease came with the arrangement: territory the Sultan could spare, revenue shared, a Western presence that served Johor's interests as much as the VOC's.

For the following decades, Batam functioned as a toll booth with good harbour facilities. Dutch factors collected transit fees, shared the proceeds with Johor, and developed the settlement into a genuine commercial node.

### Tumasik and the Sultan's Miscalculation, c. 1690

English merchants had been transiting the Straits for decades, paying Dutch fees at Batam en route to China and the broader Southeast Asian trade. By the 1690s the English East India Company had accumulated enough of a grievance to take it formally to Parliament, and Parliament made it a diplomatic matter: the Sister Republic compact was not supposed to mean English traders paying Dutch tolls indefinitely.

The Dutch response was technically correct and entirely unhelpful: the toll was the Sultan's, shared with the VOC by arrangement. Take it up with Johor.

The English did.

The Sultan of Johor saw an opportunity. Tumasik — a natural harbour at the Malay Peninsula's tip, then lightly settled — was his to give. He gave it, calculating that two Western powers positioned on opposite sides of the strait would watch each other, compete for Johor's favour, and leave the Sultan as kingmaker between them.

The Sultan did not know about the Maritime League.

His Bendahara — the chief minister who handled trade relations, received the foreign factors, and counted the ships — was the one who noticed. The English and Dutch factors were not competing; they were meeting regularly, corresponding, coordinating. The Bendahara reported what he saw. The Sultan had not divided his guests. He had given both sides of the eastern chokepoint to two allies who together now controlled it completely.

The Sultanate accepted the situation. The leases brought revenue. The Maritime League's presence deterred threats that Johor could not have handled alone. The alternative — attempting to rescind a lease from the Sister Republics in the same decade they had gone to war to defend the Republic of Meuse — was not seriously considered.

### A Century and a Half of the Strait

From the 1690s to 1850, Tumasik and Batam developed in parallel: two settlements, two flags, one strait.

Trade drove both. The eastern entrance to the Malacca Straits was the bottleneck through which the commerce of two oceans passed — Indian textiles, Chinese porcelain and tea, Javanese spices, Malay tin, the outputs of the Nusantara archipelago moving toward Europe and the Americas. Both posts accumulated the infrastructure that serious commercial traffic demands: warehouses, repair facilities, chandlers, money-changers, pilots who knew the local waters, interpreters who knew the local languages.

The two settlements developed distinct characters shaped by their parent republics. Tumasik carried an English commercial sensibility — the EIC's factor houses, English contract law, the social architecture of a settlement where status attached to commercial standing more than hereditary rank. Batam carried the VOC's more systematized approach: meticulous ledgers, warehousing organized for scale, a Dutch Reformed church alongside the Malay mosque that had predated the lease.

Both settlements were cosmopolitan in ways that their parent republics' domestic cities were not. Malay, Javanese, Chinese, Tamil, Bugis, and Arab traders all had reason to pass through or settle. Labour was recruited across the archipelago. Neither settlement was predominantly European; both were shaped by the European commercial framework imposed on a Southeast Asian population and geography.

### The Covenant Pressure and the Value of the League

Throughout this period, the Nusantara Sultanates navigated between two Western blocs. The Maritime League held Batam and Tumasik and offered naval weight and republican solidarity. The Covenant held the Philippines — Iberian colonial territory since the Spanish arrival in 1565 — and periodically tested the boundaries southward into the Sulu Archipelago and northern Borneo: raiding backed by Iberian patronage, trade disputes requiring third-party resolution, pressure on Muslim sultanates that the Covenant framed as civilising interest.

The League was the better neighbour. It did not colonize; it leased. It did not demand conversion; it traded. When Covenant pressure required a counterweight, the Maritime League's presence in the Straits was the resource Nusantara could invoke without formal submission to either bloc.

The Sultanates were not naive about this. Western commercial presence extracted its own advantages. But there was a real difference between the League's model — a trading post that paid rent and left domestic governance alone — and the Covenant's model in the Philippines. The choice, when it needed to be made, was not difficult.

### The Federation and the Federal Capital, c. 1850

The Nusantara Federation was constituted around 1850: a federation of sultanates across the archipelago, with the Lanfang Republic of West Borneo as its one republican member. The federation needed a capital on neutral ground — somewhere that did not belong to any one sultanate's sphere, that could be read as common space rather than one power's city.

The two lease territories were the answer. England and the Dutch Republic surrendered the leases; the Straits Federal District was constituted as federal capital territory. The formal transfer took the better part of a decade — property law, commercial arrangements, the status of buildings and land plots accumulated over 150 years all required negotiation. Some assets transferred to the new federal administration. Many remained in English or Dutch private hands, because private property did not transfer with a sovereign lease.

When the transfer was complete, both republics retained a formal institutional presence: the **clearing houses** — each republic's official station in the District, functioning as a combination of trade delegation, consular office, and institutional memory of the arrangement that had built the place.

### 1905 and After

The Covenant's collapse in 1905 removed the threat that had shaped Nusantara's strategic calculations for two centuries. The Philippines, liberated from Iberian rule in the cascade that followed Russia's defeat by Japan, entered a transitional period before joining the Nusantara Federation. The federation's footprint — already spanning the Malay Peninsula, Sumatra, Java, Borneo, Sulawesi, and the eastern archipelago — extended to its modern form.

## The District in 2000

The Straits Federal District in 2000 is the administrative capital of a federation of several hundred million people and among the most cosmopolitan cities in the world.

Tumasik and Batam are connected by a fixed crossing — bridge and tunnel — completed in stages through the 20th century. The District government administers both territories as a single unit, though the physical and cultural distinction between the English-heritage Tumasik side and the Dutch-heritage Batam side remains visible in architecture, street names, and institutional character.

The **English Clearing House** and **Dutch Clearing House** are among the oldest continuously operating institutions in the District. By 2000 they function as trade offices, cultural centres, and informal consulates — the two republics maintain formal diplomatic representation in Nusantara's member-state capitals, but the clearing houses predate modern diplomatic conventions and have simply continued, accreting functions as circumstances required.

English and Dutch corporations remain significant presences in the District's commercial life. The warehouse districts, the older financial buildings, much of the original harbour infrastructure: these passed through private hands across the transfer decade and were developed rather than abandoned. The economic archaeology of 150 years of foreign commercial operation is visible to anyone who looks at who owns what.

The District has no voting representation in any Nusantara member-state assembly. It is governed by a federal commission appointed by the federation's rotating chair. Its permanent residents include citizens of every member state, along with a substantial population of long-term resident foreigners — English, Dutch, and others — whose families have been in the District for generations without acquiring Nusantara citizenship.

---

## Position in the World

The Straits Federal District is neither fully of Nusantara nor separate from it. It is the federation's capital and its most international city simultaneously — a place shaped by colonial commercial infrastructure that became, through the particular history of the Maritime League and the Sultanates' strategic calculations, something neither colonial nor straightforwardly indigenous.

Its vantage point in 2000 is specific: post-Covenant, post-transfer, prosperous, cosmopolitan, and carrying in its street patterns and property records the memory of everything that made it what it is.
