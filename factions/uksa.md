# United Kingdom of South America

**Official name**: United Kingdom of South America (*Reino Unido da América do Sul* / *Reino Unido de América del Sur*)
**Capital**: Rio de Janeiro
**Existed**: circa 1840–1905
**Head of State**: King of the United Kingdom of South America (Bragança dynasty)
**Constituent kingdoms**: Kingdom of Brazil; Kingdom of Peru; Kingdom of the Río de la Plata; Kingdom of Chile
**Official languages**: Portuguese (Brazil); Spanish (Peru, Río de la Plata, Chile)
**Covenant membership**: The Covenant (member)

---

## Overview

The United Kingdom of South America was the Covenant's principal presence in the southern hemisphere — a composite monarchy constituted from the Iberian Empire's remaining South American viceroyalties following the loss of Gran Colombia to League-backed independence in the 1820s. It existed for roughly six decades before the Covenant's 1905 collapse, which it did not survive. Its successor states joined the League of Nations in the early twentieth century; the Bragança dynasty retreated to Las Malvinas, where a remnant survives to the present as the only state in the hemisphere outside League membership.

The UKSA was vast. Its four constituent kingdoms — Brazil, Peru, the Río de la Plata, and Chile — spanned both the Atlantic and Pacific coasts of the southern continent, encompassed the Amazon basin, the Andes, the pampas, and Patagonia, and contained more territory than any other state in the world save Russia. It was also, structurally, a political settlement dressed as a kingdom: an arrangement that served the internal needs of the Iberian dynasty more than it served the peoples it governed, and which produced the resentments that eventually dissolved it.

---

## The Iberian Settlement

The UKSA's constitution was an act of internal Iberian family politics as much as colonial reorganisation.

The Iberian Empire — the dynastic union of the Spanish and Portuguese crowns formed in the 1780s — was always an asymmetric arrangement. Spain was the larger partner: greater European territory, greater colonial network, the prestige of Madrid. Portugal contributed Brazil and its Indian Ocean trading legacy but found itself the junior party within a unified structure. This imbalance required management, and the Gran Colombia crisis of the 1820s provided the occasion.

Bolívar's independence campaigns succeeded in securing Gran Colombia — Venezuela, New Granada, Ecuador — as a League-recognised republic. Iberia retained the southern territories: Peru, Chile, the Río de la Plata, and the vast Portuguese-speaking heartland of Brazil. Rather than continue governing these as separate viceroyalties with distinct colonial administrations, the Iberian crown reorganised them into a single entity with its own identity and institutions. This was the United Kingdom of South America, constituted circa 1840.

The Portuguese branch of the Iberian dynasty — the House of Bragança — was given the UKSA as their domain. The logic was a concession: Spain retained European primacy, the Madrid court, and the remaining extra-South American colonies; Portugal received the largest single land mass on earth, with a Portuguese-speaking majority, as its sovereign kingdom. Both branches remained Iberian — the same dynasty, the same ultimate Covenant alignment — but each held a distinct sphere.

The Spanish crown's side of the settlement involved a genuine sacrifice. Peru and the Río de la Plata were among the wealthiest Spanish colonial territories — Potosí's silver legacy, Buenos Aires's commercial dynamism, Chile's Pacific trade position. Ceding their governance to the Portuguese branch was not an act of indifference but of calculation: better a unified Covenant monarchy capable of resisting League pressure than three separate viceroyalties that might follow Gran Colombia's example one by one. Whether the calculation was correct remained disputed in Iberian political circles for the rest of the empire's existence.

## Geography and Character

Brazil dominated the UKSA. Portuguese-speaking, vast, and the seat of the Bragança court at Rio de Janeiro, it was the kingdom's population centre, cultural heart, and political core. The Amazon basin and the Brazilian interior remained sparsely settled through the UKSA's entire existence; the Atlantic coast, from the mouth of the Amazon south to the Río de la Plata, was where the kingdom's commercial and administrative life concentrated.

The Spanish-speaking constituent kingdoms — Peru, the Río de la Plata, Chile — were the internal minority. Each had its own colonial history, its own regional aristocracy, and its own relationship with the Covenant's Commercial networks. Peru's highland economy was built on mining and indigenous agricultural labour; the Río de la Plata's pampas sustained the most productive cattle and grain agriculture in the hemisphere; Chile's narrow Pacific strip looked west across the ocean toward Nusantara and the League's Asian trade routes. All three found themselves, after 1840, governed from a Portuguese-speaking court in a Portuguese-speaking kingdom where the monarchy's prestige, cultural patronage, and administrative appointments flowed naturally toward Brazil.

The UKSA's access to both Atlantic and Indian Ocean trade — which the Iberian Pink Strip was designed to complement by providing an overland connection — gave it theoretical commercial strength. In practice, the League's maritime dominance meant that most of this trade moved through League-aligned intermediaries. The UKSA sold its exports and bought its imports through networks it did not control, which kept it commercially functional but strategically dependent on the League's goodwill even while remaining Covenant-aligned politically.

## The Covenant Years

The UKSA's relationship with the Covenant was less intimate than the Iberian Empire's had been. The Covenant had been built around European great-power solidarity; the UKSA was a South American kingdom with limited capacity to project force into European affairs and limited need for the Covenant's European military guarantees. What the Covenant provided the UKSA was diplomatic recognition, commercial solidarity, and the implicit guarantee that League powers would not support South American independence movements as they had supported Gran Colombia.

That guarantee held through the mid-nineteenth century. The League had absorbed the lesson of Gran Colombia — that supporting independence movements at the Covenant's periphery was strategically sound — but it was not unlimited in appetite for South American interventions, and the UKSA's size made direct challenge expensive to contemplate. The Guyanas Federation to the north, sandwiched between Gran Colombia and the UKSA's Atlantic coast, was the League's toehold in the region; it was kept intact by League membership guarantees and by the UKSA's recognition that absorbing it would trigger a response the Covenant could not guarantee to back.

Internally, the UKSA suppressed republican movements with the combination of patronage, censorship, and periodic force characteristic of Covenant governance. The Spanish-speaking regions produced the most persistent republicanism — Buenos Aires intellectuals reading League pamphlets, Lima students corresponding with Gran Colombian counterparts — while the Brazilian interior was largely quiescent, its political life organised around regional landowners who found the monarchy's patronage system accommodating.

---

## Internal Tensions

The Portuguese-Spanish fault line ran through every institution. Court appointments, military commissions, civil service positions, commercial licences: all flowed more readily to those with Portuguese connections and the right relationship with the Rio de Janeiro court. This was not a policy so much as a gravitational effect — power concentrated where the king lived, and the king lived in Brazil.

The Spanish-speaking aristocracies of Peru and the Río de la Plata had accepted the 1840 settlement as the price of avoiding the chaos that had consumed Gran Colombia during its independence wars. They had not accepted permanent subordination, and the distinction mattered. Buenos Aires in particular maintained a political culture in which the UKSA's constitution was treated as a negotiated arrangement that could, in principle, be renegotiated — a view that Rio de Janeiro found inconvenient and periodically suppressed without ever fully extinguishing.

The Bragança court's response to these tensions was characteristically dynastic: marriages, titles, governorships, the careful distribution of honours to regional elites who might otherwise find republican arguments compelling. It worked, until it didn't.

---

## 1905: The Collapse

Russia's defeat by Japan in 1905 dissolved the Covenant's foundational assumption — that the monarchical great powers of Europe could maintain their bloc solidarity against League pressure indefinitely. The collapse was rapid precisely because the UKSA had so little internal coherence to fall back on once external backing disappeared.

The Spanish-speaking constituent kingdoms moved first. Buenos Aires declared the Republic of Argentina within weeks of the Covenant's dissolution becoming clear; Lima followed with the Republic of Peru; Santiago constituted itself as the Republic of Chile before the year was out. Each declaration cited the cessation of Covenant obligations as releasing them from the 1840 settlement's terms — a constitutional argument of debatable validity that nobody was in a position to contest by force.

Brazil's trajectory was slower and more complicated. The Bragança court remained in Rio de Janeiro through the initial crisis, attempting to hold the Portuguese-speaking kingdom together as a rump state even as its Spanish-speaking constituents departed. The Brazilian republican movement, which had been building for decades, saw its opportunity and pressed it. The monarchy's position eroded over the following years rather than collapsing overnight. Brazil became a republic by the early 1910s, though the precise mechanism — abdication, deposition, or something more negotiated — is disputed in the successor state's own historiography.

European Iberia dissolved simultaneously. Catalonia, Euskara, Galicia, and the remaining Spanish and Portuguese territories reconstituted as separate republics. The Iberian Empire that had created the UKSA ceased to exist in any meaningful form. The Bragança dynasty, expelled from Brazil and with no European Portugal to return to, was without a country.

## The Malvinas Retreat

Las Malvinas — the islands at the southern extreme of the former UKSA's Atlantic coast — had never been of significant economic importance to the kingdom. They were administered from the Río de la Plata, sparsely settled, and notable primarily for their position athwart the shipping routes around Cape Horn. Argentina claimed them as the natural successor to the Río de la Plata viceroyalty, a claim that was legally straightforward and militarily uncontested.

The Bragança dynasty contested it anyway. With nothing else left to contest, the islands represented the only sovereign territory they could physically occupy. A small court — the king, his household, loyalist officials, and sufficient financial administrators to manage what came next — established itself at Puerto Soledad. Argentina protested. The League noted the situation. Neither took action sufficient to dislodge a dynasty that was, in practical terms, already finished.

What kept the Malvinas viable was not legitimacy but utility. See the Malvinas entry.

## Successor States

The UKSA's four constituent kingdoms produced six successor states of varying size and coherence.

**Brazil** is the largest and most populous, retaining the UKSA's capital at Rio de Janeiro and most of its administrative infrastructure. Its republican transition was the most drawn-out of the successor states, completed in the early 1910s. It joined the League of Nations in 1912.

**Argentina**, centred on Buenos Aires and the Río de la Plata basin, was the most economically developed of the successor states and the fastest to seek League membership. Its agricultural exports — beef, grain, wool — found ready markets in the League world, and the commercial relationships that had been conducted through League intermediaries during the UKSA period were formalised quickly. League membership followed within a year of independence.

**Peru** and **Chile** joined the League in the following years, each managing the transition from Covenant-era governance to republican institutions with varying degrees of difficulty. Peru's highland interior and its relationship with indigenous Andean communities gave its republican transition a particular character; Chile's geographic isolation and Pacific orientation made its transition somewhat smoother.

**Bolivia** and **Uruguay** emerged as smaller successor states from the edges of the Río de la Plata and Andean territories. Paraguay's status in the immediate post-1905 period was complicated by its geographic position and was resolved through negotiation over several years.
