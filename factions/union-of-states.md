# The Union of States

**Official name**: The Union of States  
**Capital**: Philadelphia  
**Founded**: Treaty Day, 1763  
**Government**: Federal republic  
**League membership**: Maritime League (founding non-European member, 1763)

---

## Overview

The Union of States is a federal republic occupying the eastern seaboard of North America, east of the Appalachian mountain range. Founded by treaty in 1763 at the conclusion of the Seven Years War, it is the only republic in the Atlantic world constituted through negotiation with its former metropoles rather than won by revolution. Its founding document abolishes permanent hereditary bondage — the first polity in the Atlantic world to do so at its founding.

It is, in many respects, the Maritime League made flesh: commercial, maritime, constitutionally serious, and built by people who grew up in the shadow of two working republics.

## Colonial Origins

The Union's colonial heritage is dual. English settlement of the eastern seaboard began in the early seventeenth century — Puritan communities in New England, tobacco and later rice colonies in the Chesapeake and Carolinas. Dutch New Netherland — the Hudson Valley, the Delaware estuary, the territory between — developed alongside it, separate in governance but interwoven in commerce and culture from early on.

The two traditions never collided as they might have. English and Dutch settlers traded with each other, intermarried across the colonial boundary, and built towns where the distinction was increasingly academic. The Huguenot influx after 1685 accelerated the process: skilled Protestant craftsmen and merchants fleeing Louis XIV's Edict of Fontainebleau settled disproportionately in New Netherland and New England, adding a French-speaking Protestant layer to an already mixed population and reinforcing the northern commercial economy.

By the early eighteenth century, the boundary between the English and Dutch colonial territories was a legal formality. Colonial assemblies on both sides corresponded regularly. A joint customs arrangement had been in place for decades. The two metropoles — already bound by the Compact of the Sister Republics — allowed the informal fusion to develop without interference. The bulk payment scheme, by which the colonies settled their trade obligations to England and the Dutch Republic in a combined sum divided by agreed formula, rationalized what was already practical reality.

## Geography and Its Consequences

The Union's most formative fact is its boundaries.

East: the Atlantic, its highway and lifeline. North and west: French America — Quebec and the St. Lawrence in the north, the Ohio Valley and Great Lakes arc curving westward, Louisiana reaching up from the Gulf of Mexico. South: Spanish Florida. The Appalachian mountain range marks the effective western limit: beyond it, the French fur trade empire extends indefinitely, maintained by river systems and Indigenous alliances that require no dense settlement. France has an entire continent to expand into and no reason to contest the coastal strip.

This is not a republic that expands. It is a republic that deepens.

Without a frontier safety valve, the coastal strip had to develop what it had. Land became scarce and therefore valuable. Agriculture grew more intensive — planters could not simply exhaust soil and move west. Cities grew because there was no agrarian alternative pulling people away. New Amsterdam became a genuine city by European standards: a financial centre, a publishing hub, a port of cosmopolitan density. Philadelphia, Boston, Newport, Charleston followed. Manufacturing arrived earlier than it would otherwise have: textile industries, metalworking, shipbuilding — trades that flourish where land is limited and labour is diversified.

The plantation economy of the southern states faced this constraint most acutely. Tobacco exhausts soil; in our timeline, planters moved west. Here they could not. By the early eighteenth century, the southern states were restructuring — toward crop rotation, mixed agriculture, and the early stages of manufacturing — or declining. This restructuring weakened the plantation interest before the question of chattel slavery was fully settled.

## Labor

The dominant unfree labor institution in the colonial period was indentured servitude. Men and women — predominantly European — contracted to work for a term of four to seven years in exchange for Atlantic passage, food, and lodging. The contract could be bought and sold; the conditions were often brutal; legal protections were thin. But the term ended. The servant became free.

This temporal limit — absent from chattel slavery — structured the political economy differently from our timeline. Property in persons was property for a term. The Dutch half-freedom tradition in New Netherland had always normalized a similar logic: bondage as a condition one moves through rather than a permanent identity. The two traditions reinforced each other as the colonies fused.

Chattel slavery — permanent, hereditary, race-coded — existed in the southern colonies and had been progressively entrenched in law through the late seventeenth century. But the plantation economy's geographic constraint undercut its consolidation. The slave interest in 1763 was politically real but not structurally dominant across the whole colonial economy. When the founding document abolished permanent hereditary bondage, it was abolishing an institution that had not achieved the overwhelming entrenchment it reached in our timeline.

The founding document ends the legal status of chattel slavery and establishes that no person born within the Union's jurisdiction inherits the condition of their parents' bondage. It does not abolish time-limited labor contracts — that reform proceeds through state assemblies over the following decades. The freed population of the former slave states enters a society that, imperfectly and unevenly, has long had room for people emerging from unfreedom.

## Indigenous Relations

The Haudenosaunee Confederacy had maintained a treaty relationship with the Dutch at Albany since the early seventeenth century — the Covenant Chain, a diplomatic tradition of conferences, condolence ceremonies, and mutual obligations. The English colonial assemblies had joined this tradition; the Union inherits and continues it.

The Union of States has no structural need to displace the nations it borders. The Appalachian ridgeline is the boundary: east of it, Union territory; west of it, French-claimed river systems and Indigenous homelands the Union has no appetite to contest. Border nations along the ridge are recognized treaty partners, trading in forest goods and maintaining passage through the mountain gaps.

The colonial period had its wars and its injustices — fraudulent land transactions, the displacement of smaller nations close to the coast, conflicts that left lasting wounds. But the relentless structural pressure of westward expansion — the force that made Indigenous-colonial conflict irresolvable in our timeline — is largely absent. The Haudenosaunee Confederacy in particular remains a recognized diplomatic entity with standing in the Union's world.

## The Seven Years War and Treaty Day

The Seven Years War arrived in North America as French pressure on the Appalachian approaches — a chain of forts along the Ohio Valley meant to confirm French control of the river systems and contain colonial settlement east of the mountains. For the colonies, it was a frontier crisis requiring coordinated defense that neither the English nor Dutch colonial assemblies could mount separately.

The joint command that formed under this pressure was the Union of States before it had a name. English, Dutch, and mixed colonial forces fought together, funded by assemblies that taxed themselves, supplied by logistics that had been building toward integration for decades. Metropolitan regulars from England and the Dutch Republic arrived but played a supporting role. The colonies held their frontier and pushed back French expansion from the Ohio approaches.

French America's thinness — vast on the map, sparse on the ground — showed under sustained pressure. French Canada held perhaps 75,000 settlers against a combined colonial population approaching two million. The French military position rested on fort garrisons and Indigenous alliances: formidable for raiding, insufficient for sustained campaigning against a settled population of that size.

The peace conference of 1763 included colonial delegates as parties — not representatives of the English or Dutch metropoles, but negotiators for a self-governing entity that had just funded and fought a seven-year war. They signed the treaty as the Union of States. The Sister Republics recognized them. France accepted the Appalachian boundary. The Maritime League extended membership on its standard condition: republican government, which in 1763 the Sister Republics made explicit meant a founding document abolishing permanent hereditary bondage.

Treaty Day is marked in the Union of States with deliberate civic modesty: picnics, speeches, the ringing of bells. There was no shot fired in anger, no tyrant to denounce. The republic began as a negotiation.

## Governance

The Union is a federal republic. Each member state — the former colonies, English and Dutch — maintains its own assembly, courts, and domestic laws. The Union Congress, seated in Philadelphia, handles foreign policy, defense, currency, and interstate trade. It is bicameral, with representation by both state and population.

The executive is a President of the Union, elected by the Congress for a fixed term, non-renewable beyond a set limit, and constitutionally subordinate to the Congress. The debt to Lambert's Instrument of Government is visible in the structure: no hereditary office, fixed terms, mechanisms that do not depend on any single person's virtue to function.

The franchise is propertied — land or assets above a qualifying threshold, set to include the commercial and artisan classes as well as landowners. It is not universal. The exclusions it begins with — women, the unpropertied, the freed who have not yet accumulated qualifying assets — are contested from the founding and erode unevenly over the following generations.
