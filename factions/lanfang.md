# Lanfang Republic

**Official name**: Lanfang Republic (蘭芳共和國, *Lan Fang Gongheguo*)
**Status**: Member state of the Nusantara Federation
**Founded**: 1777
**Capital**: Mandor
**Primary territory**: West Borneo interior

---

## Overview

The Lanfang Republic is the sole republican member of the Nusantara Federation, occupying the interior of western Borneo from its southern heartland around Mandor to the gold and tin fields of Monterado in the north. It is the only surviving kongsi republic — a form of self-governing mining cooperative originating among Hakka Chinese settlers in the archipelago — and among the oldest continuous republics in the League of Nations world.

Lanfang's position within Nusantara is anomalous by design. It is not a sultanate; it has no royal house, no hereditary succession, no court. Its head of state is an elected president, its legislature drawn from the kongsi membership. It sits inside a federation whose other members are all monarchies, holds a South China Sea patrol fleet that predates the federation itself, and governs a population that is part Hakka Chinese, part Dayak, and increasingly neither in any simple sense.

The republic reached its present form through a consolidation that its founders did not plan, a naval arrangement that its partners did not advertise, and a federation membership that its neighbours once considered impossible.

---

## History

### The Kongsi at Mandor (1777)

The Lanfang Republic was founded in 1777 by Luo Fangbo, a Hakka migrant from Guangdong who had reached western Borneo five years earlier. The gold fields of the interior had been drawing Chinese miners since the mid-eighteenth century; what Luo built was not a new settlement but a new political form — a unified self-governing body drawing together fourteen kongsi groups under a common framework, with elected leadership, codified agreements on resource allocation, and collective defence.

The republic's capital at Mandor placed it within the sphere of the Pontianak Sultanate, newly established in 1771 by Sultan Abdurrahman Alkadrie. Lanfang paid tribute to the Sultan and maintained the relationship carefully; Pontianak's coastal position made it a natural commercial partner and a useful buffer against external pressure. The arrangement suited both parties. Pontianak received tribute and a stable inland economy it could not have administered itself. Lanfang received political cover and access to coastal trade routes.

To the north, the older Sambas Sultanate held a competing claim over the territory around Monterado, where separate kongsi groups had organised their own federation — Fosjoen — in 1776. Fosjoen and Lanfang were distinct entities, operating under different sultans, with different memberships and different trading relationships. For two decades they coexisted without merging, the Pontianak-Sambas rivalry providing each with its own external anchor.

### Consolidation (c. 1800–1810)

The pressure that produced Lanfang's unified form came from two directions simultaneously.

From the north, the Iberian Empire's sponsorship of Sulu raiding fleets was intensifying. Sulu held formal suzerainty over much of North Borneo by virtue of a cession from a weakened Brunei, and operated with Iberian backing as a deniable instrument against League-adjacent trade in the South China Sea. Individual kongsi groups, whatever their internal governance, were poor candidates for resisting coordinated pressure from a Covenant-backed maritime power.

From the Straits, the Maritime League's factors at Batam and Tumasik were making the commercial arithmetic clear. Fragmented kongsis competed with one another for trading terms and undercut each other's prices. A unified entity could negotiate as a bloc, access the clearing houses' credit networks, and secure the kind of guaranteed pricing that individual groups could not. The League offered better terms to a consolidated partner — not as formal policy, but as the natural consequence of how its factors priced risk.

Within Fosjoen, these pressures exposed the fault lines between member groups. The Santiaogou kongsi, controlling the northern mining sites closest to Sambas, weighed its options: remain in a federation under pressure, or seek separate accommodation with the sultanate. The offer from Sambas was real, and in another world might have succeeded. Here it failed. The Dutch Republic's factors at Batam declined to back the split — a unified Lanfang-Fosjoen entity was worth more to them than a fractured landscape — and without Dutch commercial backing, the exit was not attractive enough. Santiaogou remained.

The merger of Fosjoen and Lanfang formalised what geography had already implied: a single republic spanning the full interior of western Borneo, its southern base at Mandor under Pontianak's umbrella and its northern fields at Monterado under Sambas's, paying modest tribute to both sultanates and therefore fully controlled by neither. The Pontianak-Sambas rivalry, which might have been a vulnerability, became the structural guarantee of Lanfang's autonomy. Neither sultan could absorb the republic without the other contesting it. Both accepted a formalised tribute arrangement — kept low by the very balance of power that protected Lanfang — rather than risk the gold trade routing entirely through their rival's territory.

### The Fleet and the Palawan Incident (early 19th century)

The naval arrangement grew from the same commercial logic as the consolidation. A Lanfang republic capable of patrolling the South China Sea trade routes served the League's interests without requiring the League to commit its own vessels to direct confrontation with Covenant forces. The solution was a series of ship purchases, conducted through Dutch yards at Zeeland and English yards on the Thames, on instalment terms generous enough to constitute a subsidy without being formally recorded as one. The ships were Lanfang's — purchased, crewed, and operated by the republic — and what Lanfang did with its own vessels was its own sovereign business.

What Lanfang did was patrol the South China Sea.

The fleet's primary purpose was the suppression of Sulu raiding fleets operating under Iberian backing out of the Philippine archipelago. Sulu raiders preyed on Chinese junks and League-adjacent merchantmen in the waters between Borneo and the Philippines; Lanfang's vessels intercepted, escorted, and where necessary engaged them. For southern Chinese merchant communities, the sight of Chinese ships — flying a Chinese republic's flag — maintaining security in the sea lanes registered differently from English or Dutch warships doing the same work.

The arrangement was tested in what became known as the **Palawan Incident**. A Lanfang patrol fleet, in pursuit of Sulu raiders who had attacked a convoy of merchant junks, followed the raiding vessels into waters adjacent to Palawan — within the Iberian Philippines' orbit. An Iberian warship out of Manila intercepted the patrol and ordered it to withdraw. The Lanfang commander declined.

Shots were exchanged. The Iberian vessel disengaged.

The diplomatic aftermath was controlled by a single structural fact: any formal Iberian protest required explaining why an Iberian warship had been protecting Sulu pirates from a Qing tributary defending Chinese merchant shipping. No protest was filed. The incident established, without written acknowledgement from any party, that Lanfang's patrol range was not subject to Iberian veto.

Within the League, the instalment terms on subsequent ship purchases became somewhat more generous.

### Lanfang and the Dayak Interior

The republic's relationship with the Dayak peoples of the Borneo interior predates the formal Nusantara Federation and is older than any of Lanfang's external diplomatic arrangements. Chinese miners and Dayak communities had developed working relationships since the earliest kongsi settlements: trade, labour, alliance against shared threats, and over generations intermarriage that produced communities neither straightforwardly Hakka nor straightforwardly Dayak.

The Sulu sultanate's formal suzerainty over North Borneo — inherited from Brunei through a debt of military assistance — made Dayak communities in the island's interior nominally subject to a power closely tied to the Covenant. In practice, Sulu's authority did not extend far from the coast. Lanfang's inland presence, and its demonstrated willingness to pursue Sulu-backed raiders wherever they fled, made the republic a more immediate and reliable factor in Dayak security than any sultanate.

The intermarriage communities that developed across two centuries are among Lanfang's most distinctive populations: Hakka-Dayak families whose members hold positions in both kongsi governance and Dayak longhouse leadership, whose cultural identity resists clean categorisation, and whose linguistic range typically spans Hakka, a Dayak language, and Malay. In the republic's political life these communities occupy a specific and recognised role — neither fully within the original kongsi membership structure nor outside it.

### Nusantara Membership (c. 1850)

When the Nusantara Federation was constituted around 1850, Lanfang's inclusion as the federation's one republican member required justification to the sultanate partners. The justification was the preceding half-century.

Lanfang had defended the Borneo interior against Covenant pressure at a time when the coastal sultanates could not project force inland. Its fleet had kept the South China Sea lanes open for Nusantara trade. Its Dayak alliances had stabilised the island's interior in ways no sultanate administration could replicate. Brunei, whose own North Borneo position had been eroded by Sulu encroachment, had particular reason to value a republic that had consistently checked Sulu's inland reach.

The sultanates did not accept a republic among them as an act of ideological tolerance. They accepted it because Lanfang had earned the place.

Lanfang's membership brought with it the South China Sea fleet — the only naval force among the founding members oriented outward toward the Pacific rather than along the Malacca Straits. For the next fifty-five years, as Iberian pressure from the Philippines continued, Lanfang's vessels remained the federation's forward presence on its eastern maritime flank.

### After the Covenant (1905–present)

The Covenant's collapse in 1905, triggered by Russia's defeat in the Far East and the resulting cascade of internal failures across the bloc, ended the strategic threat that had shaped Lanfang's naval role for a century. The Philippines, liberated from Iberian rule in the years following, entered a transitional period before joining the Nusantara Federation. Sulu, stripped of its Covenant patron, negotiated its own incorporation into the federation as the archipelago's political landscape was redrawn.

Lanfang's fleet, no longer required for South China Sea patrol against Covenant-backed raiders, evolved into the nucleus of what became the Nusantara Federation's maritime force. The republic retained its own naval tradition and officer class, which remains distinct within the federal structure in 2000.

## Lanfang in 2000

The Lanfang Republic in 2000 governs the western Borneo interior from its capital at Mandor, with Monterado as the republic's second city and historical seat of the former Fosjoen territory. The gold fields that founded it are exhausted — worked out over two centuries — but the republic's economy has diversified through timber, agriculture, and the manufacturing base that developed along the coastal access routes in the twentieth century.

The republic's population of several million includes the Hakka-descended kongsi communities, the Hakka-Dayak intermarriage communities, Dayak groups who live under Lanfang governance without being formally part of the kongsi structure, and a smaller number of Malay and other Nusantara citizens who settled in the interior over the federation period.

Governance follows the kongsi model adapted for a modern republic: an elected president, a legislature drawn from registered member communities, and a civil service that has absorbed considerable Dayak institutional practice alongside the original Hakka frameworks. The republic is the only Nusantara member state with an elected head of state; the constitutional implications of this within a federation of hereditary sultanates have been the subject of continuous low-level diplomatic negotiation since 1850, and are unlikely to be resolved.

The Lanfang Naval College at Mandor trains officers for both the republic's own patrol vessels and the broader Nusantara maritime force. The college's ethos preserves the memory of the South China Sea patrol era in ways that the federation's official histories do not always reflect.

## Position in the World

Lanfang occupies a specific place in the world's diplomatic imagination: the Chinese republic that survived. It is the counter-example to every argument that Chinese political culture is incompatible with republican governance, the proof that League association did not require Europeanisation, and the bridge through which League commercial norms entered southern Chinese merchant networks in the nineteenth century.

Its relationship with the League's founding republics remains structurally significant. English and Dutch commercial interests in the western Borneo interior trace back two centuries; the shipyards whose descendants still supply Lanfang's navy are among the oldest continuous business relationships in the League world. The clearing houses at the Straits Federal District hold institutional memory of the arrangement in their archives, though they do not advertise the instalment terms.

Within Nusantara, Lanfang's anomalous status is simply a fact of the federation's history — too old and too entrenched to resolve, too useful to any party to seriously attempt resolution. The republic does not press the question of its republican model as a template for the other members. The other members do not press the question of when Lanfang will acquire a royal house.

The arrangement suits everyone.
