# The English Republic

**Official name**: The Commonwealth of England  
**Capital**: London  
**Head of Government**: Lord Protector (title established by the Instrument of Government)  
**Founded**: 1649 (abolition of the monarchy); constitutional settlement 1653

---

## Constitutional Structure

The English Republic is governed under the Instrument of Government, drafted by John Lambert and adopted following the Commonwealth's transition from Cromwell's personal rule. It remains the foundational constitutional document.

### The Franchise

Voting rights are extended to men holding property or assets above a defined threshold — the original Instrument set this at £200. This encompasses landowners of middling rank and above, as well as merchants and commercial property holders. It is explicitly not limited to landed gentry, a deliberate choice reflecting Lambert's view that the commercial classes had as much stake in the Republic's stability as the rural aristocracy.

This is not modern democracy. It is a propertied republic — one that ties civic participation to a material stake in order, while excluding the landless poor and women from the franchise entirely.

### Parliament

The property-qualified electorate votes for Parliament. Parliament is bicameral in practice, though the relationship between its chambers has evolved over time. Parliament holds legislative authority and, critically, confirms the Lord Protector.

### The Lord Protector

The head of government. Unlike Cromwell's tenure, the role under Lambert's settlement is not personal rule dressed in constitutional language — it is genuinely accountable to Parliament. The Lord Protector is confirmed by Parliament, cannot dissolve Parliament arbitrarily, and governs within the Instrument's constraints.

Two provisions distinguish Lambert's Instrument from Cromwell's practice:

**No hereditary succession.** The office cannot pass by blood or appointment of the incumbent. This was Lambert's direct response to Cromwell's arrangement with his son — the precise error that nearly destroyed the Republic. The Lord Protector governs; he does not found a dynasty.

**Retirement at sixty.** Drawing on Roman precedent — the cursus honorum's age requirements for office — the Instrument requires the Lord Protector to yield his office upon reaching sixty years of age. Lambert's argument was practical rather than philosophical: a Lord Protector who retires while still capable is present to witness the succession, to steady the transition, and to ensure the mechanism functions before his death removes him from the picture entirely. The Republic's continuance should not rest on any single man's longevity.

Succession falls to Parliament, which nominates and confirms a replacement through open deliberation.

### The Army

The New Model Army retains significant political weight, particularly in the early decades of the Republic. Lambert's settlement works in part because he commands its loyalty. Over time, as the constitutional settlement matures and the Royalist threat recedes, the army's direct role in politics diminishes — though it remains an institutional presence.

## Internal Factions

### Independents
The dominant faction in Lambert's early government. Broadly tolerationist on religion, supportive of the constitutional settlement, commercially minded. Lambert himself sits within this tradition.

### Presbyterians
The conservative Parliamentary faction. Skeptical of religious toleration, favoring a more restricted settlement. Crucial to Lambert's coalition — he needs their support for stability, which requires concessions on religious policy. They accept the constitutional settlement in exchange for property protections and a degree of religious establishment.

### Commonwealthmen
Republican purists who view any executive authority with suspicion, preferring a stronger Parliament and a weaker Lord Protector. A persistent critic faction rather than a governing one.

### Royalists
Suppressed but not eliminated. Their cause loses credibility steadily as the Republic proves durable and Charles II ages in French exile. After 1672 — when Charles traveled with Louis XIV's forces against the Dutch Republic — the Royalist cause collapses in English public opinion. Supporting the restoration of a man who marched with France against England's allies is politically untenable.

## Position in the World

A founding member of the Maritime League. The English Republic's colonial presence spans the southern eastern seaboard of America and the Caribbean. Its relationship with the Dutch Republic — formalized through the Maritime League and deepened by the Compact of the Sister Republics — is the central axis of its foreign policy.
