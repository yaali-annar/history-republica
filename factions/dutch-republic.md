# The Dutch Republic

**Official name**: The Republic of the Seven United Netherlands  
**Capital**: The Hague (seat of government); Amsterdam (commercial centre)  
**Head of Government**: Grand Pensionary of Holland (de facto national leader)  
**Founded**: 1581 (Act of Abjuration); independence recognized 1648

---

## Constitutional Structure

The Dutch Republic is a confederation of seven sovereign provinces: Holland, Zeeland, Utrecht, Gelderland, Overijssel, Friesland, and Groningen. Each province governs itself and sends delegates to the States-General, the Republic's federal assembly.

In practice, Holland — the wealthiest province and home to Amsterdam — dominates. The Grand Pensionary of Holland functions as the Republic's de facto head of government, managing foreign policy, finance, and inter-provincial relations.

### The Grand Pensionary

Appointed by the States of Holland, which is composed of representatives of the province's cities and nobility. The cities hold the dominant voice, and the cities are governed by regent families — an oligarchy of merchants and lawyers who have held civic power for generations.

The Grand Pensionary serves at the pleasure of the States of Holland. The appointment is not popular in character; it is the product of negotiation among the regent class. In practice, a Grand Pensionary with strong commercial relationships and political skill can hold the position for decades — as de Witt demonstrated.

There is no hereditary claim to the role. Succession on retirement or death is handled by the States of Holland through the same appointment process.

### The States-General

The federal assembly, where each province holds one vote regardless of size or wealth. Foreign policy, war, and treaty ratification require unanimity — a rule that frustrates rapid decision-making but prevents any single province from dragging the others into conflicts they oppose.

## Internal Factions

### The Regent Class
The merchant oligarchy that governs the cities of Holland and, through them, the Republic. Pragmatic, commercially oriented, generally opposed to military adventurism that disrupts trade. De Witt's base. The regent class is not a unified faction but a social stratum with broadly shared interests.

### The Orangist Faction
Supporters of the House of Orange, who held the position of Stadtholder across most provinces and served as military commanders. In our history, the Orangists were a persistent counter-current to the regents' dominance, claiming popular and military support against the oligarchy.

In this history, the 1672 crisis — which in other circumstances broke de Witt and elevated William of Orange — did not produce the disaster the Orangists required. William III remains a figure of limited political influence. The Orangist faction persists, particularly in the land provinces, but does not dominate national politics.

### The Land Provinces
Gelderland, Overijssel, Utrecht, and others tend toward more conservative, Calvinist, and Orangist sympathies than the maritime provinces. Their interests — agricultural, military, ecclesiastical — frequently diverge from Amsterdam's commercial priorities. The States-General's unanimity requirement gives them veto power they occasionally use.

## Strategic Orientation

The Dutch Republic's defining strategic choice was made in 1641, following the joint capture of Malacca with the Sultanate of Johor. The faction arguing against continued investment in Brazil won the internal debate: the WIC wound down its Brazilian commitment while leverage remained, the VOC consolidated around Batam rather than pursuing a Java land empire, and Dutch capital and attention turned properly toward New Netherland.

The result is a Republic that is maritime and commercial in character — trading posts and carrying trade rather than territorial conquest. This orientation made partnership with the English Republic structurally natural: their interests in America were geographically divided, their interests in Asia were commercially complementary, and their shared vulnerabilities to European monarchies provided strategic alignment.

---

## Position in the World

A founding member of the Maritime League. The Dutch Republic's colonial presence includes the northern eastern seaboard of America, the Batam factory (leased from the Sultanate of Johor), and commercial networks across the Baltic, Mediterranean, and Indian Ocean. Its relationship with the English Republic — formalized through the Maritime League and the Compact of the Sister Republics — is the central axis of its foreign policy.
