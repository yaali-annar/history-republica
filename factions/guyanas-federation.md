# Federal Republic of the Guyanas

**Official name**: Federal Republic of the Guyanas
**Capital**: Paramaribo
**Founded**: circa 1795
**Head of Government**: President, elected by the Federal Assembly for a five-year term
**Constituent states**: English Guiana; Dutch Guiana (*Suriname*); French Guiana
**Official languages**: English, Dutch, French (all three co-official at federal level)
**League membership**: League of Nations (1795, founding member as successor to three Maritime League territories)

---

## Overview

The Federal Republic of the Guyanas occupies a coastal band of northern South America, bounded to the west by Gran Colombia, to the south and east by the United Kingdom of South America, and to the north by the Caribbean Sea. It is a federation of three former colonial territories — English, Dutch, and French — that constitute themselves as a single republic in the final years of the eighteenth century, drawn together by a shared Atlantic coastline, a converging colonial history, and the abrupt appearance of an Iberian Empire to their south that was larger than any of them could face alone.

The federation works, in the sense that it has persisted for two centuries and maintained its borders. Whether it works in any deeper sense is a question its citizens debate with energy and without resolution. Its population is among the most diverse in the League world — layered by slavery, indenture, escape, and indigenous survival across four centuries of colonial contact — and its politics are organised primarily along communal lines that most politicians deplore and none have managed to dissolve. The external threat to the south has always been more reliable as a unifying force than anything internal.

## The Three Territories

The three territories that form the federation have separate colonial origins and different rhythms of development, which is why the union took until the 1790s to become possible.

**English Guiana** is the westernmost territory, bordering Venezuela. English settlement dates from the early seventeenth century, with the characteristic pattern of the English Republic's colonial presence: a coastal plantation strip dependent on enslaved labour, governed by a colonial assembly of planters, connected to the metropolitan economy by the sugar trade. The English Republic's commercial character meant the colonial assembly had real power, which made it both self-governing and resistant to metropolitan pressure on the slavery question.

**Dutch Guiana** (*Suriname*) occupies the central stretch of coast. The Dutch presence dates from the same period. The plantation economy here was, if anything, more intensive than in English Guiana, and produced one of the more unusual features of the Guyanese interior: the Maroon communities. Beginning in the seventeenth century, enslaved persons escaped the plantations into the rainforest and established independent communities — the Saramaka, Ndyuka, Matawai, and others — which fought the colonial government to a standstill and were eventually recognised by treaty. The Dutch Republic, whose constitutional tradition was more comfortable with negotiated autonomy than with military suppression of people asserting their own freedom, ratified those treaties with more consistency than the colonial administration that produced them. The Maroon communities have maintained their interior territories under recognised treaty rights since the 1760s and entered the federation as a distinct, legally acknowledged presence rather than an absorbed population.

**French Guiana** is the easternmost territory and the snag. The French colonial administration held it as a sparsely settled territory of less commercial significance than the Dutch and English portions — smaller population, less developed plantation economy, more dependent on the metropolitan government than the self-governing English and Dutch colonial assemblies. When France went republican in 1789, French Guiana's status changed overnight. The planter class that had held authority under the royal administration found itself without a patron; the republican institutions that replaced them were improvised and unstable. French Guiana arrived at the federation negotiations as the junior and least-organised partner.

## Abolition and the Indenture Years

The abolition of slavery in the Guyanas was not a clean break. It was a protracted substitution — one form of coerced labour replaced, gradually and imperfectly, by another — and the communities it produced remember that history differently from each other, which is one reason Guyanese politics remains what it is.

The Dutch Republic's slow move away from slavery through the eighteenth century applied pressure on Dutch Guiana, where the plantation economy resisted the trajectory its metropole was following. Abolition arrived in stages: the slave trade first, then the institution itself, with transition periods and compensation arrangements that served the planter class more than the enslaved. English Guiana followed a similar pattern under similar metropolitan pressure from the English Republic, whose Calvinist and commercial conscience had long been uneasy with permanent hereditary bondage.

The gap between abolition and a free labour market was filled by indenture. Plantation owners who could no longer hold enslaved persons recruited indentured workers on five-to-seven-year contracts — predominantly from South Asia, and to a lesser extent from the southern Chinese republic and elsewhere in the League world. The contracts promised passage, wages, and return transportation. The passage was usually kept; the return transportation often was not. Indentured workers who remained after their contracts — whether by choice, by debt, or by the simple impossibility of affording a passage home — became another permanent layer of the Guyanese population.

The result is a society in which three broad groups share a coastline and the memory of exploitation, but not the same exploitation: the Afro-Guyanese came through slavery; the Indo-Guyanese came through indenture; the Maroon communities fought for their freedom while the others were still waiting for legislation. Each community's sense of its own suffering is genuine. Each community's sense that the others do not fully recognise it is also genuine. The argument has not been settled in two hundred years and shows no sign of settling.

## The Federation

The federation crystallised in the mid-1790s under the pressure of two simultaneous events: the French Republic's emergence made French Guiana's alignment with its neighbours finally possible, and the formation of the Iberian Empire gave all three territories a reason to align immediately rather than at leisure.

The English and Dutch territories had been moving toward closer integration for decades. They were both Maritime League members, their colonial assemblies corresponded regularly, and the commercial logic of treating the Guyanese coast as a single economic zone was apparent to anyone who traded along it. What had prevented formalisation was French Guiana: under the French monarchy, the territory was aligned with a Covenant-adjacent power rather than the League, and a two-territory federation that excluded the eastern coast would have been geographically incoherent.

The French Republic changed that. French Guiana's new republican administration sent delegates to Paramaribo within months of France's own founding. The Iberian Empire's formation, roughly contemporaneous, concentrated minds. The Iberian Empire held Brazil to the south — a territory so large that it appeared on maps as an entity the three Guyanas together could not meaningfully contest. The calculation was not subtle: separately, any one of the three territories was a manageable problem for a determined southern neighbour; together, they were a League member with a recognised border and obligations attached to it.

The **Federal Compact of 1795** constituted the three territories as a single republic. Each retained its own domestic assembly, courts, and legal traditions. Foreign policy, defence, and currency became federal matters. The federal capital was established at Paramaribo, the largest and most centrally located city on the coast, which the Dutch territory's assembly accepted with the understanding that federal institutions would be linguistically neutral.

Gran Colombia, constituted in the 1820s to the federation's west, became the natural friendly neighbour the federation had not yet had. The two republics share a border along the Venezuela-English Guiana frontier that has never been a point of serious contention, and the commercial and political relationship between them has been warm for two centuries. Guyanese politicians who need an example of a successful South American republic point to Gran Colombia; Gran Colombian politicians who need a League ally in northeastern South America point to the Guyanas.

## Society

The federation's population falls into several communities that interact continuously and integrate incompletely.

**The Afro-Guyanese** are the descendants of enslaved Africans brought to the plantations across all three territories. They are the federation's largest community by origin, concentrated in the coastal towns and the agricultural zones behind them. Their political organisations were the first to form after abolition and the most consistently engaged with the federal institutions; they have also been the most consistent advocates for a federal identity that supersedes communal politics, partly because their numbers and early organisation made federal identity advantageous to them and partly from genuine conviction.

**The Indo-Guyanese** are the descendants of South Asian indentured workers, concentrated most heavily in English Guiana and Dutch Guiana, where the indenture system ran longest. Agricultural in occupation for the first generation, they have diversified extensively since. Their internal organisation is partly religious — Hindu and Muslim communities alongside Christian — and partly linguistic, with Hindustani maintaining a presence alongside English and Dutch in the territories where the community is largest.

**The Maroon communities** of the Dutch Guiana interior hold a status unlike any other in the federation: recognised autonomous territories under treaties that predate the federation itself and were written into the Federal Compact without modification. They govern their own communities by their own traditions, send observers to the federal assembly, and maintain a specific legal right to be consulted on any legislation affecting the interior. They are not fully part of the federation's political system and do not claim to be.

**The indigenous peoples** — Arawak, Carib, Wapishana, Warao, and others along the coast and in the interior — have a more fragmented legal status than the Maroon communities, their treaty arrangements having been made at different times with different colonial administrations. The federation has been negotiating a consolidated indigenous rights framework since the 1870s without completing it.

**The European communities** — English, Dutch, and French descendants of the colonial settler and administrative class — are a small minority by the twentieth century, concentrated in professional and commercial occupations in the three main coastal cities. Their political weight is less than their economic weight, though the two are not unconnected.

## Constitutional Structure

The Federal Republic is governed by a **Federal Assembly**, which elects the President for a non-renewable five-year term. The Assembly is composed of delegates from the three constituent territories in proportion to their populations, with a minimum representation guarantee for French Guiana, which would otherwise be perpetually underrepresented given its smaller size.

Each constituent territory — English Guiana, Dutch Guiana, and French Guiana — maintains its own **Territorial Assembly** with authority over domestic matters: land, education, internal courts, and local taxation. Federal authority covers defence, foreign affairs, the currency, and inter-territorial trade. The division has produced a federal government that is constitutionally robust and practically underfunded, since the territories guard their domestic revenue jealously and the federal budget depends on their contributions.

The three official languages carry equal status at the federal level in principle; in practice, English and Dutch predominate in federal administration, and French Guiana's political class has been arguing about this since 1795. The argument is more symbolic than operational — federal bureaucrats are routinely trilingual — but symbols matter, and the argument has never been fully resolved.

## Position in the League

The Guyanas Federation joined the League of Nations as a founding member, succeeding its three constituent territories' individual Maritime League memberships. Its League membership is, in a practical sense, the reason the federation exists: without the mutual defence guarantee that League membership provides, the calculation about Brazil's size would produce a different conclusion.

The federation's contribution to League councils is modest in material terms — it has no navy of significance and its economy, while functional, is not large. Its value to the League is partly geographic (a stable League presence on the South American Atlantic coast between Gran Colombia and the UKSA) and partly symbolic (one of the few successful multi-lingual, multi-ethnic federal republics in the League world). The League has an interest in the Guyanas working, which means the Guyanas have access to League diplomatic and financial support that its size would not otherwise justify.

The relationship with Gran Colombia is the federation's most important bilateral relationship within the League. The two share a border, a Caribbean coastline, a common interest in South American stability, and a long history of managing the consequences of the UKSA's size. League meetings that concern South American affairs are typically preceded by Guyanese-Gran Colombian consultations that the other parties have learned to treat as a joint position paper.
