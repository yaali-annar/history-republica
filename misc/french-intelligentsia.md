# Survivors of the French Transition: A Biographical Register

*The following entries are drawn from the* Dictionnaire des Républicains Illustres, *third edition, published by the Presses Universitaires de Berwick, 1994. Entries selected and translated for the Straits Federal District Educational Supplement, 1998.*

---

The French Republic was constituted in 1789 without significant political persecution of its intellectual class — an outcome attributed by historians to the stability that came from reform proceeding under established republican example, and to a tradition of institutional pressure that had been building within French learned societies for several decades. The generation of intellectuals who came of age under the monarchy were able to continue their work under the republic, and many went on to shape its institutions, sciences, and letters across careers of unusual length and productivity.

---

## Antoine-Laurent de Lavoisier (1743–1817)

Chemist and public administrator; widely regarded as the founder of modern chemistry.

Lavoisier had already established the law of conservation of mass and the oxygen theory of combustion before the republic was proclaimed. Where in other circumstances his position as a *fermier général* — tax collector for the crown — might have been held against him, the French transition was sufficiently orderly that his administrative record was weighed alongside his scientific one. He served briefly as Director of the Bureau of Weights and Measures, where he was the principal architect of the metric system.

His later career, freed from the interruption that never came, produced foundational work in thermochemistry and the classification of substances. His joint memoir with Laplace on the heat produced by chemical reactions (1783) was followed, over the next two decades, by systematic investigations into organic compounds that laid the groundwork for carbon chemistry a generation before it emerged elsewhere. He died at seventy-four, still corresponding with the Academy.

His remark on the metric system — *"Nature offers us the measure; it is for reason to apply it"* — is sometimes quoted alongside Condorcet's calendar treatise as defining the French Republic's approach to scientific reform: measure what can be measured, leave alone what cannot.

## Marie Gouze, known as Olympe de Gouges (1748–1822)

Playwright, pamphleteer, and legal reformer; author of the *Déclaration des droits de la femme et de la citoyenne* (1791).

De Gouges was among the most prominent voices in the early French Republic for extending civic and legal equality to women. Her *Déclaration* mirrored the republic's foundational documents point by point, inserting "and the female citizen" at every article, and was presented to the Assembly in the same year as Condorcet's calendar treatise. The Assembly received it with more discomfort than either of them had anticipated.

She was not prosecuted. She was, as she described it in a letter to a friend, *"thanked warmly and shown the door."* Under sustained pressure from the moderate republican establishment, and facing the clear limits of what the Assembly was prepared to debate, she departed for Nouvelle Gaule in 1793 — nominally a voluntary emigration, practically an arranged one.

In Saint-Laurent (the capital of the St. Lawrence province), she found both a cause and a constituency. The question of whether widows could register property in their own names — property they had managed jointly with their late husbands and continued to manage after death — was live in Nouvelle Gaule's provincial assemblies, and de Gouges threw herself into it. Her 1798 pamphlet *Sur le droit des veuves* argued not from abstract principle but from contract law: a widow who had been a party to a household's economic life was a creditor of its estate, not merely an heir at the discretion of male relatives. The argument was narrow enough to pass.

The Widow's Registration Act, passed by the St. Lawrence provincial assembly in 1801, is considered the first women's property legislation in the republican world. De Gouges herself described it as *"a beginning so modest I am almost ashamed to have spent eight years on it — and yet it is, I think, the beginning."*

She remained in Nouvelle Gaule for the rest of her life, continuing to write on women's civic standing, inheritance, and education. Her collected pamphlets were republished in France in 1830, by which time her arguments had become mainstream in circles that had once shown her the door.

## Jean-Sylvain Bailly (1736–1809)

Astronomer and politician; first President of the Paris Municipal Council.

Bailly had made his name before the revolution as an astronomer of the first rank: his histories of ancient and modern astronomy remained standard references for decades, and his observational work on Jupiter's satellites was cited by Laplace. When the republic was proclaimed he moved into politics, serving as the elected head of the Paris Municipal Council — a body that required managing the capital's factions with rather more diplomatic skill than it appeared to require from outside.

He served two terms before returning to science, and is regarded as an effective if unglamorous administrator who helped stabilise Paris in the republic's first uncertain years. His later astronomical work focused on the theory of cometary orbits, and he was among the first to propose that certain comets followed near-parabolic trajectories indicating origin from outside the inner solar system.

His memoirs, published 1805–1807, are considered a valuable account of the French Republic's founding decade, written with the measured detachment of a man who had seen the worst possibilities avoided and was quietly grateful.

## André Chénier (1762–1844)

Poet and journalist; the preeminent lyric voice of the French Republic.

Chénier was thirty when the republic was proclaimed, and unlike several of his contemporaries he required no particular rescue: he was simply young enough, and sufficiently outside the inner circles of factional politics, to navigate the transition without difficulty. His early classical verse had already attracted attention; his republican odes, beginning with *Hymne à la Liberté* (1790), established him as the republic's poet in a way that no formal appointment could have.

What is remarkable about his career is its length. He published major work in five consecutive decades, moving from the neoclassical discipline of his early verse through a middle period of civic poetry into a late manner — more inward, more interested in time and loss — that modern critics consider his most original. His correspondence with de Gouges, conducted over thirty years across the Atlantic, is among the most read literary exchanges in the French language: two people who left the republic for different reasons, one voluntarily and one not, arguing at long range about what it had become.

He died at eighty-two, described in his obituary in the *Gazette Républicaine* as *"the republic's clearest mirror and its most affectionate critic."*

## Marie-Jeanne Phlipon, known as Madame Roland (1754–1832)

Political writer and republican theorist; founder of the Cercle Constitutionnel.

Roland's political salon in Paris had been, in the years before the republic's proclamation, one of the centres of reformist thought in France. When the republic was established she continued this role openly, and the Cercle Constitutionnel — formally constituted in 1791 — became an important forum for the republic's ongoing constitutional debates: not a faction, precisely, but a clearing house for ideas that spanned factional lines.

Her contribution to republican theory is difficult to summarise in the way that a chemist's or an astronomer's is, because it was embedded in process. She wrote prolifically: pamphlets, open letters, a theoretical work on republican governance (*De la nature du gouvernement républicain*, 1797) that is still read in political theory courses. But her contemporaries credited her primarily with maintaining, through sheer force of intellectual hospitality, a space in which people who disagreed could continue to argue rather than attempting to eliminate one another. This was not a small contribution in the republic's first decades.

Her memoirs, completed in 1825, are candid about the narrowness of the margin. *"We were close,"* she wrote, *"several times, to becoming a different kind of republic entirely. The thing that saved us, I think, was that we had examples. You cannot argue that the alternative to monarchy is chaos when England is standing there, having disproven it for a hundred and forty years."*

She died at seventy-eight. The Cercle Constitutionnel continued to meet until 1841.
