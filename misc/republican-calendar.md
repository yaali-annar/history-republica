# On the Reform of the Civil Calendar

**Marie Jean Antoine Nicolas de Caritat, Condorcet**
*Presented to the Assembly of the French Republic*
*Messidor, Year 143 — June 1791*

---

## I. The Defects of the Present Calendar

The Gregorian calendar, which the Republic has inherited from the monarchy and the Church, is not merely a religious instrument — it is also a defective one. Its months are of unequal length, a fact without astronomical or mathematical justification, which descended to us from Roman political accident. The days of the week do not correspond to fixed positions within the month; a merchant who wishes to know whether the fifteenth of next month falls on a market day must consult a table. The leap day falls in February by historical contingency, not by design, creating an asymmetry that propagates through the year.

These are practical defects. They impose real costs on commerce, on public administration, and on the calculation of wages. A laborer paid monthly cannot verify, without arithmetic, whether his employer has given him his due; the months are not equal. A notary scheduling a term cannot know, without a calendar in hand, whether the date falls before or after a day of rest.

The republic inherits these defects. It need not keep them.

## II. Against the Metrification of Time

Before setting out a positive proposal, I must address a school of thought that has gained some currency in the discussions of the Commission: the proposal to apply decimal division not only to the calendar but to time itself — to divide the day into ten hours, the hour into one hundred minutes, the minute into one hundred seconds.

I oppose this with some force.

The proposal confuses two distinct categories of reform. The civil year is a conventional instrument, a coordination device for agriculture, commerce, and civic life. Its structure is arbitrary and historical, and may be improved by better arbitration. But the division of the day into hours and minutes is not a convention of the same kind. Every clock and sundial in the republic, and in every republic with which we correspond, divides the day into twenty-four parts. The reform of this system would require the replacement of every instrument of time-measurement in France — not gradually, but simultaneously, on the day of adoption. The English Republic, which has governed itself without a monarch for one hundred and forty-two years, has not been found deficient in rationality on account of its hours. Nor has the Dutch Republic. Nor have the Union of States.

More fundamentally: the week — the *septade* — is not an arbitrary convention ripe for reform. It is the oldest and most universal coordination device of human society, found in every civilization we know. It persists not because Sunday was commanded at Sinai, but because human beings require a regular rhythm of labor and rest, and seven days has proved, across all latitudes and all centuries, to be the interval that best balances productive work with the restoration it requires. To replace the septade with a period of ten days is not to reform the calendar — it is to impose an experiment in social engineering on a people who have not asked for one.

The republic is new. Its legitimacy rests on the consent of the governed, not on the power to reorganize their lives. We should reform what is defective. We should not reform what works.

## III. The Proposed Structure

The calendar I propose proceeds from a single arithmetical observation: twenty-eight is divisible by seven. A month of twenty-eight days contains exactly four weeks, with no remainder. Twelve such months yield three hundred and thirty-six days; thirteen yield three hundred and sixty-four. With one day added outside the structure of months and weeks — a civic day, falling on the first of March — the common year reaches three hundred and sixty-five. In leap years, an intercalary day falls on the twenty-ninth of February, equally outside the structure of months and weeks, bringing the count to three hundred and sixty-six.

This structure has several properties that recommend it above all alternatives I have examined.

First, the calendar is *perennial*: every date falls on the same day of the week in every year. The first of Germinal is always a Primidi. The merchant, the magistrate, and the laborer may dispense with their almanacs for the purpose of knowing on what day a given date falls.

Second, the correspondence with the Gregorian calendar is *exact and permanent*. The Republican year opens on the second of March and closes on the twenty-eighth of February. Every date in the Republican calendar corresponds to the same Gregorian date in every year without exception. No historical record requires re-dating. No treaty needs retranscription. The reform is additive, not disruptive.

Third, wages and terms calculated by the month are *automatically equal*. A worker paid a monthly salary receives the same number of days' labor in each period, without exception and without dispute.

## IV. The Names of the Months

The names of the months should be intelligible to the citizen without learning, and should mark the progress of the year through nature rather than through the succession of emperors and priests. I propose names drawn from the seasons and phenomena of the temperate year. The thirteen months, from the second of March:

**Germinal** — the germination of seeds; early spring.
**Floréal** — the flowering; middle spring.
**Prairial** — the meadows; late spring.
**Solaire** — the approach of midsummer; the solstice falls within this month.
**Messidor** — the grain harvest; early summer.
**Thermidor** — the height of heat; high summer.
**Fructidor** — the fruit harvest; late summer.
**Vendémiaire** — the grape harvest; early autumn.
**Brumaire** — the mists; middle autumn.
**Frimaire** — the frosts; late autumn.
**Nivôse** — the snows; early winter.
**Pluviôse** — the rains; deep winter.
**Ventôse** — the winds; late winter, before the renewal.

The civic day, falling on the first of March, I propose to call the *Jour Républicain*: a day outside the count of months and weeks, belonging to the republic entire. It falls between the last day of Ventôse and the first day of Germinal — between the final breath of winter and the first act of spring.

## V. The Epoch

Every calendar requires a beginning. The question is not whether to choose a date, but which date most honestly represents what this calendar is.

It would be easy — and false — to begin the count from the proclamation of the French Republic. Easy, because it would anchor the calendar in our own moment; false, because it would imply that republican government began in France, which it did not.

The first modern republic was constituted in England. In the year one thousand six hundred and forty-nine, the Commonwealth of England was established by an act that ended the reign of the monarchy. The second of March of that year — the first morning of what would become Germinal — I propose as Year One, Day One of this calendar.

This choice serves several purposes. It honours a republic that has governed itself, without monarchy, for one hundred and forty-two years, and whose example demonstrated that self-government was possible before France was in a position to attempt it. It makes the calendar a *republican* calendar rather than a *French* calendar — a distinction of considerable importance if we wish our sister republics to adopt it. And it gives the calendar a depth of history that no calendar anchored to the present year could possess.

The French Republic does not begin republican time. It continues it.

## VI. Conclusion

The reform I propose preserves what works: the septade, the Gregorian correspondence, the existing instruments of time. It corrects what is defective: the inequality of months, the variability of day-of-week by date, and the awkward placement of the leap day within the year. It honours what is true: that republicanism is not a French invention, but a human achievement of which France is, as of this year, the newest and most populous exemplar.

I commend it to the Assembly.

*Condorcet*
*Messidor, Year 143*
