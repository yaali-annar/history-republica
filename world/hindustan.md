# Hindustan

## The Subcontinent and Its Name

"Hindustan" — from the Persian *Hind* and *stan*, the land beyond the Indus — is a geographic designation without a political referent. The Mughal Empire used the word to describe the territories it governed; the world outside used it to describe the territories the Mughals approximately covered. In neither usage did it imply unity beyond the administrative kind, and when the Mughal administration ceased to cover much of anything, the word remained while the structure it had named dispersed into its constituent parts.

In 2000, Hindustan is the term that appears on League of Nations maps for the subcontinent between the Indus and the Bay of Bengal, from the Himalayas to Cape Comorin. It contains eight sovereign states. It does not contain any country called India.

---

## The Mughal Decline

Aurangzeb's death in 1707 set in motion what his reign had made inevitable. He had spent fifty years reversing the administrative accommodations through which his predecessors had governed a diverse subcontinent — taxing religious minorities, dismantling the coalition of Hindu and Muslim elites that the imperial revenue system depended on, fighting campaigns in the Deccan that consumed the treasury and the army simultaneously — and left behind a court in Delhi capable of performing succession ceremonies while effectively governing nothing outside its walls.

The inheritors were not a vacuum but a set of existing powers that the Mughal system had suppressed without eliminating. The Maratha chiefs had spent a generation conducting mobile campaigns across the Deccan against Aurangzeb's forces; the campaigns had ruined the Mughal army and produced a Maratha military class whose fighting capacity was the best-tested on the subcontinent. The Nawabs of Bengal, Awadh, and Hyderabad exercised independent authority over the most productive regions while maintaining the polite fiction of Mughal delegation. The Sikh communities of the Punjab, forged into a martial brotherhood through a century of conflict, held territory the Mughal governors had stopped being able to take back. Mysore's rulers — Hyder Ali from the mid-century and his son Tipu thereafter — administered a state that had never been fully Mughal in character and saw no reason to pretend otherwise.

Five Mughal emperors in as many years after Aurangzeb's death demonstrated the pattern: the dynasty retained prestige; the power was elsewhere. The Delhi court continued for decades, receiving ambassadors and issuing *farmans* that the recipient powers filed respectfully and disregarded practically, until even the ritual maintenance became too expensive to sustain. The empire's last effective extent had been the city walls.

What filled the space was not a single successor but a competitive field of regional powers, each operating within its own cultural and geographic logic, none capable of consolidating the whole.

## The League's Presence

The European commercial companies had been embedded in the subcontinent's port cities for a century before the Mughal collapse made its political consequences visible, and the nature of that embedding shaped what followed.

The English East India Company held factories at Surat, Madras, and Calcutta — each operating under licence from the relevant authority, each functioning as a warehouse and commercial hub for the trade between the subcontinent's manufactures and the maritime world. The Dutch **Vereenigde Oost-Indische Compagnie** held positions at Pulicat, Masulipatnam, and Cochin. The French **Compagnie des Indes** had established itself at Pondicherry and Chandernagore. These were trading presences, not administrative ones: the factories were licenced, the licences were renewed, and the territory under the walls of Fort St. George and Fort William remained, legally, the sovereign land of the states that had licenced it.

The distinction was not a legal technicality. The successor states that emerged from the Mughal collapse were coherent powers with armies and revenue systems of their own. The Maratha Confederation at its mid-century peak held more territory than most European states and had proved its military capacity against the greatest Mughal army ever fielded. Mysore's rulers spent their careers improving their fortifications, training their infantry on European lines, and equipping their forces with rocketry that gave them a genuine military edge over neighbours who underestimated them. These were not polities into which a trading company could expand by accumulating jurisdiction in a vacuum. There was no vacuum.

The companies accordingly remained companies. Their factories grew into substantial cities — Madras and Calcutta acquired populations and commercial infrastructure that dwarfed the original English grants — but they grew through trade and services rendered, not through jurisdiction acquired. By the late eighteenth century, what the League's member republics held in Hindustan was a network of leased commercial ports: economically enormous, diplomatically significant, and territorially modest.

France's entry into the League in 1789 and the reform of the Compagnie des Indes on republican lines changed the character of the commercial presence without changing its territorial extent. The strategic competition that had previously set English factors against French factors in adjacent positions gave way to something more resembling a coordinated commercial front. Pondicherry, which had been France's bridgehead in a contest for influence it could not openly prosecute, became a conduit for republican ideas into the subcontinent's reforming courts. Tipu Sultan — who had been corresponding with the French Republic since 1787, seeking alliance against pressures from the north and west — found in Pondicherry's new republican administrators interlocutors genuinely interested in what Mysore was building, rather than merely calculating what Mysore might offer against a competitor.

## The Subcontinent's Divisions

The reason no successor state consolidated Hindustan is geographic and linguistic before it is political.

South of the Vindhya and Satpura ranges that cross the subcontinent's waist, the population speaks languages of the Dravidian family — Tamil, Telugu, Kannada, Malayalam — whose relationship to the Sanskrit-derived languages of the north is comparable to the relationship between Basque and French: scholarly contact across the boundary, no common root. The Dravidian south absorbed Sanskritic religious culture, court forms, and commercial vocabulary across two thousand years without being linguistically remade in return. Its states looked outward to the Indian Ocean trade and southward toward the Straits' maritime networks, and their political traditions predated the Mughal enterprise and survived it intact.

North of the ranges, the picture was complex without being unified. The Maratha heartland of the upper Deccan was Indo-Aryan in language but Deccan in political character — the Maratha houses had built their identity through military resistance to the Mughals, and that identity distinguished itself explicitly from both the northern court culture and the Dravidian south. Bengal was a world defined by its rivers: the most commercially productive region of the Gangetic plain, the subcontinent's point of maximum contact with maritime trade, and the home of an intellectual culture that would prove, across the nineteenth century, more receptive to republican ideas than anywhere else in the north. The Punjab was shaped by the Sikh tradition — a religious formation that had spent a century under existential pressure into something with strong communal solidarity and institutions of collective decision-making built into its own religious practice. The Rajput kingdoms of the western desert maintained the warrior aristocratic cultures that the Mughals had found it more useful to co-opt than to defeat.

The former Mughal heartland — the upper Gangetic plain, the cities of Delhi and Agra and Lucknow — was the subcontinent's most culturally significant zone and, by the mid-eighteenth century, its most politically fragmented. The great Nawabs held territory and courts; their courts produced some of the finest architecture, music, and literature of the century; their political position rested on administrative structures the Mughals had built, stripped of the imperial framework that had once given those structures their coherence.

Persian was the common administrative language of the northern states — the medium in which correspondence between courts was conducted, the language in which educated men across religious lines could read each other. Sanskrit served religious and classical scholarship across the whole subcontinent. English became the working commercial language of the coastal port cities, carried by the EIC's presence and the practical needs of trade. No single language served the whole.

## States in 2000

The eight sovereign states of Hindustan in 2000 represent the consolidation that two centuries of constitutional development, commercial pressure, and diplomatic realignment produced from the post-Mughal field.

**The Maratha Federation** (*Maratha Sangh*) is the subcontinent's most populous and geographically extensive state, covering the Deccan plateau and extending across western India to the coast. Its constitutional form emerged gradually from the Maratha Confederacy's existing structure: the great houses — Scindia, Holkar, Bhonsle, and Gaekwad — retained their territories while a **federal parliament** (*Mahasabha*) and elected presidency replaced the Peshwa's coordinating role. The transition occupied most of the nineteenth century; settling the terms by which great houses would accept constitutional constraint on their individual authority was slow work, and the League's admission committee did not accept the Federation's membership application until the 1870s, when a constitutional revision produced institutions the committee judged genuinely representative. Pune serves as the federal capital. The Maratha Federation is the largest economy in the League's Hindustan membership and the subcontinent's dominant diplomatic weight.

**Bengal** constituted itself a republic in the early nineteenth century, driven by the commercial energy of its river cities and the reforming impulse of a Calcutta intellectual class that had sustained contact with European republican ideas through the EIC's presence for generations. The Bengali reform movement produced, across several generations, a constitutional framework that abolished hereditary privilege, established elected representation, and — under sustained pressure from the League's French membership — legally abolished caste discrimination in public life. The social reality changed more slowly than the legal provision, as it did everywhere on the subcontinent where similar measures were adopted. Bengal's League membership dates to 1843, the earliest of any Hindustan state. Calcutta, which grew from an English factory grant into a city of several million over two centuries, was formally returned to Bengali sovereignty in 1812 while retaining the commercial infrastructure and English-language commercial culture it had built. The English Republic maintains a clearing house there still.

**Mysore** is the subcontinent's most distinctively French-influenced state, though the description understates the extent to which its character was shaped before France arrived. Hyder Ali and his successor Tipu built an administratively centralised, militarily modern state in the Deccan's southern reaches that surrounding powers found alarming. Tipu's correspondence with the French Republic — conducted through Pondicherry and sustained through the revolutionary period — produced a relationship that shaped Mysore's political evolution through the following generation. The republic that emerged in the 1820s retained the administrative capacity Tipu had built while replacing its monarchical form with elected institutions. Mysore holds League membership from 1831 and maintains the closest bilateral relationship with the French Republic of any Hindustan state; Pondicherry, a French treaty port within Mysore's coastal territory, remains the physical expression of that connection.

**The Sikh Commonwealth** (*Khalsa Raj*) holds the Punjab and the upper Indus plain — the subcontinent's northwest and the corridor through which Central Asian powers have historically pressed south. The Khalsa, the Sikh martial brotherhood, consolidated under Ranjit Singh's leadership in the early nineteenth century from a confederation of commanders into a single territorial state. The Commonwealth's constitutional form draws on the Khalsa's own tradition of collective decision-making: the *Sarbat Khalsa*, the assembly of the community, provided a precedent for representative governance that the state's reformers developed into formal institutions across the mid-century. Its external alignment was contested for decades — Russia's pressure from the northwest made studied neutrality the Commonwealth's diplomatic posture through the mid-nineteenth century. The 1905 collapse resolved the ambiguity: League membership followed in 1907.

**Hyderabad** occupies the south-central Deccan — a Nizam's state whose Muslim ruling dynasty governs a predominantly Hindu population across some of the subcontinent's most agriculturally productive territory. Its court culture is one of the finest living expressions of the Mughal synthesis; its constitutional form is the Northern Realm model — a constitutional monarchy with an elected legislature and defined limits on the Nizam's authority — rather than a republic. It joined the League of Nations as an associate in 1912 and achieved full membership in 1934, when a constitutional revision satisfied the League's criteria. France's position on Hyderabad's caste provisions remained a point of formal objection until 1951, when domestic legislation met the standard France had insisted upon. Its admission remains the most negotiated of any Hindustan member's.

**Travancore** and the **Tamil Confederacy** round out the subcontinent's League membership in the south. Travancore — on the Malabar coast, Malayalam-speaking, with a maritime tradition that long predated the League's arrival — joined the League in 1856 after a constitutional revision that formalised what the *Maharani*'s council had informally practised for a generation. The Tamil-speaking southeast coast, whose commercial relationship with the Straits trading networks is the oldest in the subcontinent, is represented in 2000 by the Tamil Confederacy, a federated republic of four former kingdoms constituted in 1887. The Confederacy's commercial centre, Madras — originally a small English factory grant, now a city of millions — was returned to Tamil sovereignty in stages across the early nineteenth century; the English Republic's clearing house there operates from a leased plot within the city it once administered.

The **Gangetic Principalities** — the former Mughal heartland's successor states, centred on Awadh and distributed across the upper Gangetic plain — are the subcontinent's most diplomatically complicated zone. Several are constitutionally organised; none has achieved League membership. The principalities' ruling cultures are predominantly Muslim, their populations predominantly Hindu, and the League's pressure on caste provisions — while not formally applicable to non-members — is read in these courts as a proxy for broader demands on internal social arrangements they have not chosen to accept. Lucknow, their cultural centre, remains one of the world's cities: its Urdu literary tradition, its musical forms, and its architecture represent a synthesis of Persian and Indic culture that the Mughal period produced and the principalities have sustained. Their relationship to the League is one of careful commercial engagement without political integration.

## The Northwest

Afghanistan lies outside Hindustan's political boundaries and has always shaped them.

The Afghan kingdoms — the Durrani Empire's successors, controlling the mountain passes and the territories between the Indus plain and Persia — were the subcontinent's primary point of contact with the Covenant's Central Asian pressure. Russia's southward expansion through Central Asia was the dominant strategic fact of the northwest frontier across the nineteenth century, and the Afghan kingdoms navigated it in the way smaller powers navigate large neighbours: with enough cooperation to avoid absorption and enough independence to maintain governing authority. Covenant alignment, in the Afghan case, was less ideological than geographic — the relevant power was Russian, the relevant threat was annexation, and the relevant response was calculated accommodation.

The 1905 collapse left the Afghan situation unresolved in ways that remain visible in 2000. The patron whose proximity had structured the kingdoms' external alignment was discredited without being replaced. League diplomatic outreach — patient, commercial, accompanied by economic relationships that Russian alignment had made difficult — has been partially successful: Afghanistan maintains a League trade agreement and sends observers to League councils without having applied for membership. The Khyber Pass, which separates the Sikh Commonwealth's territory from the Afghan highlands, is the subcontinent's most watched border, though the post-1905 generation has been considerably quieter than the century that preceded it.
