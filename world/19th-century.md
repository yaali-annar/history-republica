# The 19th Century

## The Americas Remade, 1810s–1840s

The first half of the nineteenth century settled the Americas into the shape they would hold through the twentieth.

Gran Colombia was the decisive event. Simón Bolívar's campaigns, beginning in the second decade of the century, produced a federation of three constituent republics — Venezuela, New Granada, Ecuador — that the Iberian Empire spent the better part of two decades attempting to reverse. The Covenant's Colonial Protocol committed Prussian advisors, Russian gold, and Iberian naval forces to the reconquest effort. It failed, for reasons that became the standard post-mortem of the Covenant's military adventurism: terrain that favoured defenders, supply lines that stretched from Cádiz to the northern coast of South America, and the political miscalculation of providing a diverse independence movement with a common enemy sustained long enough to harden into a common identity. Bolívar's advance was halted before the Andean interior — Iberia held Peru and the southern Andean colonies — but north of the line that emerged, the Iberian Empire never recovered ground.

Mexico followed in the 1830s through a mechanism more political than military. The Iberian strategic plan — to use New Spain as a source of northern pressure on Gran Colombia, stretching Bolívar's attention and supply — never materialised in practice. The forces were committed in principle and never arrived; the colonial administration received orders it could not execute; and Nouvelle Gaule and Canadia, whose interests were directly served by a republican neighbour to their south, provided through commercial channels and trading networks the kind of covert support that both governments maintained they were not providing. When Iberia chose to concentrate its available military capacity in the south rather than fight on two fronts, Mexico's independence movement found the space it needed. Recognition came through intermediaries, with provisions that Mexico spent the following decades negotiating away.

The Mesoamerican territories — the former captaincy-general stretching from Guatemala through the isthmus — joined Mexico in stages across the 1830s and 1840s, the affiliation negotiated rather than imposed, each constituent member calculating that federation with an established republic was a more durable arrangement than independent small-state survival.

The Northwest Territory — the vast interior west of the North American continent, home to indigenous nations whose territorial relationships with one another were complex and ancient — acquired its League-facing form in the same period. Canadia and Nouvelle Gaule, attending to what Russian Alaska on their northwestern flank implied, fostered a framework in which the indigenous nations could present a recognised territorial face to the outside world. It was not a republic in any standard sense. It was an arrangement that worked.

Canadia itself had separated from Nouvelle Gaule early in the century, and the cause was a calendar.

The French Republic's adoption of the Republican Calendar — reorganising the week, eliminating Sunday as a legal day of rest, inserting five days outside the weekly cycle — had been accepted in Nouvelle Gaule's southern territories with the pragmatism of people for whom the metropolitan government was distant. The St. Lawrence settlements were not pragmatic about it. The Catholic Church required an unbroken seven-day cycle for Sunday observance; the Republican Calendar broke it. The calendar was the final instance in a long pattern of Nouvelle Gaule's central government imposing secular republican culture on communities whose Catholicism was not a private sentiment but the organising structure of their year, their week, and their daily life. Canadia's secession was formally conducted and peacefully concluded. The two republics remained friendly neighbours, the border between them a legal fact that commerce treated as a minor inconvenience.

## Nitrates

The natural sodium nitrate deposits of the Atacama Desert had been known since before European arrival. Indigenous peoples of the high desert had extracted saltpeter from the caliche — the rough mineral crust — for generations. The Spanish colonial administration noted the deposits without urgency; the uses were limited and the quantities required were small.

What changed was European agricultural science's understanding of the soil.

Justus von Liebig — working at Giessen, in the Republic of Rhine, with the systematic rigour that the Rhine's university culture had been developing since the early century — established in the 1820s that nitrogen was the limiting factor in plant growth: the element whose absence determined crop yield more than any other, whose depletion over years of continuous cultivation produced the exhausted soils that drove peasant misery and aristocratic ruin across the continent. The agricultural revolution that had been improving yields through technique had reached its ceiling, and the ceiling was nitrogen.

The Atacama deposits were the only economically viable source of fixed nitrogen at agricultural scale. The world needed them. Iberia had them.

The discovery of what this meant to Iberia was not sudden. It accumulated through the 1830s and 1840s as the international trade in Chilean nitrates grew from a specialty to a necessity. By mid-century, League agricultural economies were importing the mineral in quantities that made the trade's political implications legible to anyone who cared to look. Iberia looked.

The price-setting that followed was never crude — a blunt embargo would have been self-defeating, since Iberia needed the revenue as much as the League needed the nitrates. What Iberia practised was something more sophisticated: prices calibrated to extract maximum revenue without provoking the League into responses that would cost more than the revenue gained. Individual members were played against one another; commercial terms were tied to diplomatic concessions; access was contingent on behaviour in League councils. The English Republic, whose access to Indian saltpeter gave it some alternative for explosives manufacture, was treated differently from France or the Republic of Rhine, which had none. The Dutch Republic's extensive Pacific commerce created pressure points of a different kind. Iberia had, for the first time since the Covenant's formation, an instrument of leverage over the League that did not require military force.

It was not export prohibition. It was something more durable: the permanent knowledge that the price could rise, the access could narrow, and the calculus was always Iberia's to set.

## The League's Response

Formal diplomacy failed in the way that formal diplomacy tends to fail when one party controls something the other cannot do without. League councils produced resolutions; Iberia received them, acknowledged them, and continued as before. The republic of Gran Colombia, whose dependence on nitrate fertilizers for the Llanos agriculture was acute, pressed the League's executive most urgently. France and the Rhine pointed at their own agricultural dependency. The Union of States, whose compact geography had produced intensive farming from the outset, found its model under strain.

What the formal channel could not achieve, the informal channel was asked to attempt.

The governor of the Chilean province — appointed by Iberia, theoretically loyal to it, and possessed of the administrative authority that controlled what left the ports — was a man whose personal circumstances had grown complicated. The details of the arrangement that followed were never committed to paper by the parties who made it, and the League's official position in 2000 remains that it has no knowledge of the events in question. What is known is the outcome: nitrate shipments that should not have cleared Iberian customs did, documented as guano — the bird-dung fertilizer that also moved through the same ports on the same ships, required no special licences, and had the additional property of discouraging thorough inspection. The guano manifests covered shipments whose contents were rather more agriculturally concentrated than the documents suggested.

The governor of Chile was eventually recalled. He did not return to Iberia.

## Brazil

The League's second instrument of pressure was more direct and considerably more dangerous.

Brazil was the Iberian Empire's largest and most valuable colonial territory: vast, populous, and sustained by an enslaved labour force that in the mid-nineteenth century numbered in the millions. The Iberian Empire had never abolished slavery; the Colonial Protocol's logic ran the other way, treating the maintenance of existing colonial structures as a condition of the Covenant's collective commitment. Brazil was the most visible manifestation of what that commitment meant in practice — and it was the most visible reminder, to the abolitionists of the League world, of what Covenant victory would mean for the hemisphere.

John Brown was born in Connecticut in 1800, in a Union of States where permanent hereditary bondage had been illegal since before his father was born. He grew up in a republic whose founding had been premised on the impossibility of governing through consent while maintaining a legal status that foreclosed consent entirely. He took this at face value in a way that his society found, at various points, admirable and alarming in roughly equal measure.

The League world's abolitionist cause had no domestic institution to oppose. What it had was Brazil.

Brown had been in correspondence with Brazilian independence networks and Ayitian republican thinkers for more than a decade before he arrived in the country in the early 1860s. His contacts were not primarily enslaved people — the communications barrier made that difficult — but the free Black population of Brazil's cities, the educated mixed-race class whose position in colonial society gave them both the grievance and the means to organise around it. What Brown brought was outside resources, tactical thinking shaped by the Appalachian irregular warfare tradition, and a willingness to be personally present in a situation that most of his correspondents in Europe preferred to fund from a safe distance.

What followed was not one rebellion but a series of connected uprisings across several provinces, poorly coordinated, sustained by the network Brown had helped build but not controlled by any single hand. The colonial military response was overwhelming by local measures and insufficient at the scale the problem demanded. Iberia was simultaneously managing the Chilean province situation, the Gran Colombia border, and the general post-Covenant anxiety about what the League's proxy operations implied for the rest of its colonial territories. The Brazilian uprisings did not succeed in their immediate military objectives. They tied up resources, damaged infrastructure, killed overseers and administrators, and made clear to every enslaved person in every province that the situation was not permanent.

Brown was captured in 1863. The trial that followed was conducted with the seriousness Iberia reserved for threats it genuinely feared, which meant it was long, heavily documented, and closely watched by the League press. His execution was delayed three times by diplomatic noise from the League members whose governments had no official knowledge of his activities. He was hanged in Rio de Janeiro in 1864, at the age of sixty-three, having spent two years in custody making arguments that the colonial administration found it increasingly difficult to answer.

The League's official condolences to his family were brief. The unofficial memorials were rather more extensive.

## The Synthetic Question

The covert operations bought time. What would end Iberia's leverage permanently was a different chemistry.

The problem was well-defined: atmospheric nitrogen is inert, constituting four-fifths of the air, entirely unavailable to plant life without being fixed into a compound the soil can absorb. The Atacama caliche fixed it geologically, over millions of years, in concentrations that could be extracted and shipped. What the League needed was to fix it industrially, at will, without Iberian permission.

The scientific foundations had been developing since Liebig's work on nitrogen in the 1820s. The Republic of Rhine's chemical industry — the most concentrated in the League, working in the same tradition that Liebig had established — was already the world's leader in synthetic chemistry by the 1850s. France's stable republican government had never lost Lavoisier to the political violence that might have claimed him; his extended work through the early decades of the century had accelerated the theoretical foundation on which the Rhine's chemists built. The two traditions were in regular exchange, in the way that institutions with open borders and shared intellectual culture tend to be.

The catalytic reduction of atmospheric nitrogen — fixing it to hydrogen under high pressure with an iron catalyst — was the solution that emerged from this tradition in the 1870s. The principle had been understood theoretically for some time; what required solving was the engineering of vessels capable of sustaining the pressures needed and the identification of catalysts that worked reliably enough to be industrially useful. The Rhine's chemical firms, which had been developing industrial pressure chemistry for decades and had a specific interest in solving the problem, solved it.

The first synthetic nitrogen plant at industrial scale came into operation in the early 1880s. By the end of the decade, several more had followed, in France, in the Rhine, and in the English Republic. The price of League agricultural nitrogen began to fall in ways that Chilean caliche, however efficiently produced, could not match.

Iberia's leverage did not collapse overnight. The existing trade continued; the infrastructure was built; habits of procurement changed slowly. But the direction was legible. Iberia had spent four decades extracting rent from a monopoly position. The monopoly was ending.

## The Weakening

The last quarter of the nineteenth century was the Covenant's long decline before the fall.

Russia's position had never been primarily about nitrates — its interest in the Covenant was geopolitical, oriented toward the Bosphorus, toward Korea and Manchuria, toward the maintenance of a buffer of monarchist states between itself and the republican world order. These ambitions were not exhausted. But Russia's internal structure — an absolute monarchy governing a vast territory through an administrative apparatus that had not substantially reformed since Peter — was increasingly mismatched to the demands the twentieth century would make of it. The conflict with Japan, when it came, would make the mismatch undeniable.

Austria and Prussia had spent a century managing their competition within the Covenant's framework, which required each to accept the other's survival as a constraint on its own ambitions. The constraint held, but the internal logic of two great powers sharing a bloc they each expected to dominate eventually would not hold indefinitely.

Iberia's position was the most complex. Its colonial revenues — once enormous, now declining as Brazil's instability mounted, as Chile's nitrate income was undercut by synthetic alternatives, as the administrative cost of holding a colonial empire without Covenant military backing grew clearer — could not sustain the metropolitan state at the level the Covenant's commitments required. The Spanish and Portuguese components of the empire had always been held together by dynastic logic and commercial interest rather than cultural fusion; as commercial interest contracted and dynastic logic became insufficient, the seams of the unification showed.

The Covenant that faced Japan in 1905 was not the Covenant that had formed in 1800. Its resources were diminished, its internal coherence was strained, and its capacity to project force to the Pacific theater — where Russia's conflict with Japan was decided — was not what any of its members had calculated it would need to be.

Russia lost. The shockwave was what everyone had predicted and no one had fully prepared for: a great power defeated by a non-European state, the Covenant's military mystique shattered, and every internal pressure that the Covenant's coherence had been holding down surfacing at once.

The century ended there, in practice, though the calendar still had five years left to run.
